02 The market-cap race

The gap to Asia collapsed — but did Canada get overtaken?

Through 2024–2026 the headlines said AI-heavy Taiwan and Korea overtook Canada by stock-market size. On the WFE's own month-end series the answer is more precise: the TMX–Korea gap collapsed from roughly US$1.3 trillion in early 2024 to just US$54 billion by May 2026 — near-parity, but on this exchange-level instrument no crossover has yet happened. The two accounts differ because they count different things; both are true, and on either basis the convergence is undeniable.

TMX has not yet reported June 2026, so its line ends at May; the June figures for Korea (4.89T) and Taiwan (4.70T) both sit below TMX's May level, so nothing in the data yet shows either passing it.

03 The drought is not universal

Other markets kept their on-ramps open.

Switch the indicator to Listed companies or step through the presets and a pattern emerges: the collapse in new listings is far steeper in Canada than in the Asian markets it likes to benchmark against. Korea ran a steady 60–160 IPOs a year through the whole window; Hong Kong swung with the cycle but recovered to 114 in 2025; even the volatile Nasdaq-US settled back at 157. The TMX went to single digits and stayed there.

04 The read

A distinctively Canadian problem — not a global one.

The WFE data reframes the diagnosis. The thinning of Canada's public-market on-ramp is not a wave that hit everyone equally; it is unusually severe here. On market cap the TMX has grown (US$2.2T in 2010 to US$4.6T in 2025), but that growth is concentration and fund product, not a widening base of newly-public operating companies. The comparators that kept their on-ramps open — and how — are the subject of the international chapter.
