What "listed companies" counts. WFE listing totals are all-in — they include ETFs, closed-end funds and other exchange-traded products wherever the exchange reports them (WFE's 2025 TMX total is 3,701, TSX + TSXV combined). That is a different universe from this report's canonical 672 TSX operating issuers (May 2026), which deliberately strips funds and products. Use the WFE count for internationally-comparable totals; use 672 for the operating-issuer decline story — the two are not meant to match.
basis & reconciliation
Two ways companies raise equity. Beyond IPO proceeds, WFE splits total equity capital raised into money raised at IPO and money raised afterwards by companies that are already listed — follow-on financing (rights issues, bought deals, private placements, at-the-market offerings). On the TMX this secondary market is the real engine: in most years follow-ons raise ten to a hundred times the IPO proceeds. The Follow-on capital button charts it; Avg IPO size derives proceeds ÷ IPO count per exchange-year. Korea and Nasdaq-US do not report follow-on capital to the WFE — their follow-on lines read as no data, not zero activity. KRX does not report the line to WFE, but Korea's own KSD depository data shows listed-market paid-in capital increases (유상증자) of ₩15.16tn (2023) and ₩21.95tn (2025) — a back-series that is not chart-filled here because it exists only in KSD HWP attachments and a government indicator page, documented in the vault for a future fill.
financing indicators & definitions
Five derived indicators. Beyond the raw WFE series, the explorer computes five ratios — labelled derived, each shown only where both input series exist for that exchange-year (no mixed-basis fill). Turnover velocity (value traded ÷ market cap × 100) is the flagship — how much of a market changes hands in a year; its 2024 values reconcile exactly with the report's canonical velocity chart (Korea ~201 · Taiwan ~124 · Japan ~117 · NYSE ~96 · TMX ~64). Avg company size (market cap ÷ listings) exposes Canada's missing large-cap tier — the average TMX company is ~US$1B against ~US$15B on the NYSE. Follow-on share, avg IPO size and foreign-listing share complete the set. Two obvious derivations are deliberately absent: average trade size (the WFE extract carries no number-of-trades series) and implied net attrition (the all-in listing count mixes ETP creation into a delisting residual — see the drawer).

Data notes. WFE annual statistics, 2010–2025, canonical 25-exchange USD file. The five newest venues — Shanghai, Shenzhen, India's NSE, Singapore and Saudi's Tadawul — carry full 2010–2025 coverage. Two exchange-level caveats: China is split across Shanghai and Shenzhen (combined they would rank second only to the two US venues), and India's NSE understates the country's true float because BSE India is not in the WFE set. LSE Group's WFE series ends in 2020 (WFE stopped receiving LSEG's returns); we gap-fill London for 2021–25 from the London Stock Exchange's own statistics (Main Market + AIM December factsheets and the LSEG monthly market reports, retrieved July 2026) — listed companies, market cap, IPO proceeds, follow-on capital and value traded, plus the derived ratios — all rendered dashed with hollow markers to flag the different provenance. GBP figures are converted to US dollars at Bank of England rates (flows at the annual average, market cap at year-end). Scope is London Main Market + AIM only. The 2020→21 step is partly a perimeter and basis break, not a market move: the pre-2021 WFE "LSE Group" figure bundled Borsa Italiana (Milan, which left for Euronext in Apr 2021) and was euro-stamped on a different basis, so the spliced series changes perimeter exactly at the splice. The one London series not extended is the IPO count — the Main Market factsheet reports all new admissions (IPOs + introductions + transfers), not an IPO-only count, so a clean IPO-only continuation can't be built from the primary files. Individual Nordic venues (Stockholm, Copenhagen, Helsinki, Tallinn) are partial; prefer the Nasdaq Nordic aggregate. Market-cap and value-traded coverage is thinner than listing counts for a few venues (lines drop where WFE has no figure). No 2026 annuals are published yet, so the series ends at 2025.

02 The market-cap race

The gap to Asia collapsed — but did Canada get overtaken?

Through 2024–2026 the headlines said AI-heavy Taiwan and Korea overtook Canada by stock-market size. On the WFE's own month-end series the answer is more precise: the TMX–Korea gap collapsed from roughly US$1.3 trillion in early 2024 to just US$54 billion by May 2026 — near-parity, but on this exchange-level instrument no crossover has yet happened. The two accounts differ because they count different things; both are true, and on either basis the convergence is undeniable. The ~US$0.5–0.6 trillion level gap between the bases is a measurement difference — Bloomberg's narrower security universe and free-float weighting against WFE's full domestic market cap of every listed company — not the junior board: the TSX Venture adds only ~US$0.1 trillion (~2%) to the WFE TMX figure, so stripping it out would still leave WFE well above the country-level count.

02b The other basis

The same race, counted by country.

The chart above ranks exchanges. The 2026 crossover headlines were written on a different instrument — Bloomberg's country-level count, a narrower, roughly free-float, primary-listing universe. On that basis Canada sits in a tight cluster with Taiwan and Korea near US$4.4–4.5 trillion, and both passed it in 2026. This is a snapshot, not an explorer — a single moment (late April 2026) to place the two accounts side by side.

03 Concentration, decomposed

The overtake is real — and it is, to a striking degree, one company.

The gap to Asia really did close. But decompose the comparators' growth and the overtake narrows to a single fact: it is, to a striking degree, one company. Roughly half of Taiwan's entire market-cap increase since early 2024 is TSMC alone — its weight in the benchmark TAIEX climbed from ~30% to ~42% as it out-grew the rest of the exchange roughly five to one. Korea is two chaebol groups; Singapore is three banks. Canada, by contrast, is barely single-name concentrated at all — its top-heaviness is a composition problem, not one dominant stock.

The honest read. The convergence with Asia is genuine — but it is not a broad-based re-rating. Taiwan's climb is overwhelmingly TSMC and the AI-semiconductor complex (add MediaTek, +~200% in H1 2026, and Hon Hai). Korea is scarcely more diversified — Samsung's group is 38.55% of the market and Samsung + SK together 65.86%: two groups, two-thirds. Singapore's record run was bank-led — DBS, OCBC and UOB are ~51.5% of the Straits Times Index and all three beat it in 1H 2026 — though the STI is a 30-stock benchmark, not the whole SGX, so there is no clean share-of-SGX figure to quote. Against those, Canada's TSX is only moderately concentrated in single-name terms (Royal Bank 6.2%, top-5 19.5%, TSX operating cap, May 2026); its problem is composition — banks and resources with a thin mid-cap growth tier — not one dominant stock.
Taiwan did not pass Canada with a deeper market. It passed Canada with one company. The overtake is an AI-semiconductor story wearing an exchange's name.

04 The drought is not universal

Other markets kept their on-ramps open.

Switch the indicator to Listed companies or step through the presets and a pattern emerges: the collapse in new listings is far steeper in Canada than in the Asian markets it likes to benchmark against. Korea's IPO count swung between 33 and 159 a year — cyclical, never collapsing — through the whole window; Hong Kong swung with the cycle but recovered to 114 in 2025; even the volatile Nasdaq-US settled back at 157. The TMX went to single digits and stayed there.

05 The read

A distinctively Canadian problem — not a global one.

The WFE data reframes the diagnosis. The thinning of Canada's public-market on-ramp is not a wave that hit everyone equally; it is unusually severe here. On market cap the TMX has grown (US$2.2T in 2010 to US$4.6T in 2025), but that growth is concentration and fund product, not a widening base of newly-public operating companies. The comparators that kept their on-ramps open — and how — are the subject of the international chapter.
