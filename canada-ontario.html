◇ The profile · Canada & Ontario

The ground the argument stands on.

Before the report pivots to the gap, the diagnosis and the fix, it is worth stating plainly what is already here: a G7 economy, a deep and under-used pool of household and institutional capital, and a decade's worth of enacted growth-supporting programs. This page is the report's scale reference — who we are, what we hold, and what we've already built. It states the levels; the chapters that follow argue the trajectory.
C$1.247T
Ontario's 2025 nominal GDP · roughly two-fifths of Canada's ~C$3.06T
A sub-national economy the size of a mid-sized OECD member state — C$1,246.7B for full-year 2025 (Ontario Economic Accounts). The base every percentage and dollar figure in this report is computed against. It is not a small place; that is precisely what makes the scale-up and retention failure a policy choice, not an inevitability of size.

A demographic inflection worth flagging. Both populations edged down in Q1 2026 — Canada −0.25%, Ontario −0.34% — the first national quarterly decline in StatCan's modern population-estimate series, as immigration policy tightened. It shifts the labour-supply and long-run-growth assumptions every forecast on the following pages rests on. Real Ontario GDP growth is also decelerating: +2.4% (2023) → +1.6% (2024) → +1.3% (2025), with an actual −0.3% q/q contraction in Q4 2025 (OEA).
GDP per capita — the level here, the trajectory next door. Nominal output per person is roughly C$74,000 for both Canada and Ontario (2024). This page states the level; the slide is the Prize page's argument — Canadian real GDP per capita has fallen to 71.4% of the US (PPP, 2024), from 83.1% a decade earlier (part of the recent slide reflects rapid population growth in the denominator, treated on the Prize page). 04 Who invests here · the household & investor base

A deep, real-estate-heavy household balance sheet.

This is the fullest on-site treatment of the investor base — the households and the wealth that domestic-capital policy actually has to work with. The pattern is consistent: substantial net worth, but tilted hard into real estate and away from productive financial assets, and mostly held through intermediaries rather than directly. The "so what" — the idle room, the ownership chart — lives on the Gap and Public-Markets pages; the base itself is here.

06 What's already working

Retention is possible — it's just not yet routine.

The report is not arguing from a blank page. Canada's one replicable public-market success is the serial-acquirer model — Constellation Software and its Topicus/Lumine spin-outs, plus Descartes — proof the TSX can host and compound a world-class name, even if it is concentrated in one corporate lineage. And where domestic capital actually shows up, companies stay: StackAdapt, a profitable Toronto scale-up, took an OTPP-led US$235M round and stayed independent — the cleanest live proof the domestic pool can anchor a champion.

The full roster — 15 domestic champions, a Nortel cautionary precedent, and three international comparables — and the four cross-case patterns are the Champions page's. This is the one-line version: the model works; the task is to make the exception the rule. The champions →

07 The policy scaffolding already in place

What we've already built — an inventory.

No other page carries this: a consolidated inventory of the enacted, live growth-supporting programs, typed by level (federal / provincial) and side — capital-side programs move money into and through the system; innovation-side programs build R&D, IP, talent or regulatory infrastructure. This is an inventory, not an evaluation: what has been built, and at what scale. Whether it converts is the Recommendations page's judgment to make.

The capital-side spine, escalating

Federal fund-of-funds — government tranche, C$ millions · BDC · 2013 → 2026-27
The anchor fact underneath it. Canada's VC market is structurally BDC-anchored: BDC Capital was an LP in private funds representing 62% of the capital actively invested in Canada (2023), with AUM of CAD 6.9bn (2024), up from CAD 0.8bn (2011). The fund-of-funds lineage above is the closest Canadian parallel to the UK's Mansion House approach.

08 The prize / the stakes

What all this scale is worth converting.

The levels above are the "before" state. The stakes are the Prize page's to argue in full — but in one line: Canadian living standards have slid to 71.4% of the US (PPP, 2024) from 83.1% a decade earlier; Ontario invests 2.76:1 in housing over intellectual property; and the IMF puts the internal-trade prize at roughly 7% of real GDP (~C$210B long-run) from eliminating interprovincial barriers. 09 The honest tension

Strong endowment, weak conversion.

Every figure on this page cuts two ways, and the report is candid about it from the start. Canada and Ontario score at or near the top of the world on the inputs to innovation and near the bottom on the outputs. The endowment is not the constraint; the machine that turns it into scaled, retained, publicly-listed champions is. That turn is argued in full on Strengths; here is the tension stated plainly.
