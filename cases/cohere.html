01 The capital journey

Toronto domicile, an increasingly Canadian lead — but foreign-strategic dollars.

Cohere raised ~US$1.47B across its named rounds. The first two lead cheques were foreign (Index, Tiger Global); from 2023 Canadian institutions stepped up to lead — Inovia, then PSP, then Radical — yet the recurring large strategic investors (NVIDIA, AMD, Oracle, Salesforce, Cisco) are all foreign.

02 The defining moment
Domicile kept — financing sovereignty not.
Cohere demonstrates both halves of the domestic-capital thesis at once. PSP leading a US$500M round (2024) and HOOPP entering (2025) show that Canadian pension capital can be mobilised behind a flagship company when the ecosystem will exists — the exact behaviour the vault argues Canada under-supplies. Yet the largest recurring cheques across the C/D/E rounds came from foreign strategics — NVIDIA, AMD, Oracle, Cisco, Salesforce, Fujitsu — because the compute and cloud partners frontier AI requires are all foreign.
Why it mattered: Cohere kept its headquarters and core R&D in Toronto — the inverse of the leakage pattern — but could not be financed domestically alone. Its retention rests on founder choice plus a sovereign-AI brand identity that aligns commercial interest with Canada's strategic interest — data-sovereign, private-deployment AI is a feature customers pay for, a more durable anchor than personal preference.

03 The policy lens

What keeps a foreign-funded champion Canadian.

Cohere is a stay, so the lens is which retention factors are replicable levers — and where the vulnerabilities sit.

Sovereign-AI positioning replicable — demand-side
Private, data-sovereign deployment is a product feature regulated buyers pay for. Government procurement of domestic sovereign AI deepens commercial embeddedness — a demand-side lever.
Pension / institutional mobilization replicable — supply
PSP led the US$500M Series D; HOOPP entered in 2025. Mobilizing more domestic institutional capital to anchor frontier companies is Lever ① Supply.
No domestic public-market anchor the open vulnerability
No TSX listing and an undecided IPO venue mean no domestic institutional holders are embedded. A US-only IPO would weaken Canadian anchoring — Lever ④ Liquidity & TSX competitiveness.
Strategic-acquirer concentration latent — federal, not provincial
Deep ties to NVIDIA / AMD / Salesforce / Cisco make each a plausible acquirer. Investment-review sits with the federal government (ISED); no provincial securities lever.
LEVER ① · SUPPLY + DEMAND
Mobilize domestic capital, then anchor the demand
Pension/institutional capital that leads frontier rounds, plus government-as-first-customer procurement that makes the sovereign-AI brand a durable Canadian tie.
04 Replicability verdict

A stay — but a qualified one.

Stayed · foreign-funded
Domicile retained; financing sovereignty was not.
Cohere is Canada's clearest case that a frontier company can keep its HQ and R&D at home — and that Canadian pensions (PSP, HOOPP) and AI-specialist VCs (Radical, Inovia) can be mobilised to lead. But its growth equity is still majority foreign and strategic, it has no domestic public-market anchor, and its strategic investors are plausible acquirers. The replicable levers are demand-side procurement of sovereign AI and deeper domestic growth capital; the risks are the undecided IPO venue and the foreign cap table.
Cohere kept its address in Toronto. It has not yet been able to keep its cap table Canadian.
