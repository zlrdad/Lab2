Nortel

Failed & dismantled
Canada's largest company at its 2000 peak — then a dot-com crash, an accounting scandal and a 2009 insolvency, ending in a US$4.5B patent fire-sale to a foreign-led consortium. The landmark value- and IP-leakage cautionary tale.
~C$398B
peak market capitalisation · September 2000
Then the largest company in Canada — over 35% of the entire S&P/TSE 300 index. ~94,500 staff worldwide; ~US$4B/yr R&D.

01 Rise and fall

A 114-year-old champion, unwound in eight.

Unlike the venture-funded startups elsewhere in the roster, Nortel's is a public-market story — of index dominance, a governance failure, and a public collapse that scattered the operating businesses and, finally, the IP.

1895–1998 · The industrial champion
Northern Electric → Nortel Networks
Incorporated in Montreal as the manufacturing arm of Bell Telephone of Canada; renamed Northern Telecom (1976); "Nortel" brand (1995); the Bay Networks acquisition (1998) signalled a data-networking pivot.
Sep 2000 · Peak
Market cap ~C$398B
>35% (up to 36.5%) of the TSE 300 · ~US$30B revenue
Index concentration meant every pension, mutual fund and index product tracking the Canadian market was, by construction, heavily exposed to a single company.
2001–2004 · Collapse & scandal
Restatements · SEC + OSC
The dot-com telecom-equipment crash, then a 2003–04 accounting scandal — earnings management via reserve accounts — forced restatements for 2000–2003. Criminal fraud charges later ended in acquittals (Jan 2013): disclosure failure devastated shareholders without a criminal conviction.
Jan 14 2009 · Insolvency
CCAA / Chapter 11
The largest corporate failure in Canadian history at the time. Operating units were sold piecemeal (2009–10) to Ericsson (~US$1.13B CDMA/LTE), Avaya (US$900M), Ciena (~US$774M), Hitachi and others.
Jun 2011 · Patent fire-sale
~6,000 patents US$4.5B
Sold to the "Rockstar" consortium (Apple, Microsoft, Ericsson, Sony, RIM, EMC), outbidding Google's ~US$4.4B. Canadian-invented IP, foreign-owned. Total liquidation proceeds reached ~US$7.3B.

02 The defining moment
The patent auction — Canadian IP, sold whole to foreign strategics.
In a week-long New York bankruptcy auction in June 2011, Nortel's ~6,000 patents and applications — wireless, optical, internet and semiconductor IP, much of it developed at the Ottawa Carling campus — sold for US$4.5B to Rockstar Bidco. Google opened at US$900M and was raised out at ~US$4.4B. Only one consortium member (RIM/BlackBerry) was Canadian; the portfolio was assigned to a US holding entity and later used to sue Google and Samsung.
Why it mattered: there was no Canadian company, pension consortium, or sovereign vehicle positioned to bid. The country's most strategic residual asset was monetized for foreign buyers rather than recycled into the domestic innovation base — the archetypal value-leakage pattern.

03 The policy lens

What would have changed the outcome.

The collapse is over-determined — a cyclical crash and a governance failure and the absence of any retention mechanism. Separating the threads maps each to a lever.

No founder or strategic control the mirror of Shopify
A widely held company had no controlling shareholder to resist board-driven over-expansion or steer the downturn — Lever ③ Retention (governance frameworks contemplating durable control).
No domestic acquirer for the IP the sharpest gap
At insolvency, nothing stood ready to bid for ~6,000 patents. A standing pension/sovereign IP vehicle to bid for distressed strategic assets sits under Lever ① Supply.
Index concentration risk systemic exposure
One stock at >35% of the index put indexed pension and retail capital on a single company's failure — pension-portfolio concentration awareness.
IP-retention policy gap no screen
Canadian-invented IP can transfer wholesale in a bankruptcy auction with no domestic-retention screen — strategic-asset screening for distressed-IP sales.
LEVER ③ + ① · RETENTION & SUPPLY
A buyer of last resort, and a reason to stay
Governance that permits durable control, and a standing domestic vehicle to bid for distressed strategic IP.
04 The lesson

Over-determined failure — but the losses fell on Canadians, and the IP left.

Failed
A company can fail for ordinary reasons; the policy questions are distribution and retention.
Nortel could have failed for cyclical reasons alone. What the case teaches is why the losses landed so heavily on Canadian pensioners — index concentration exposed ~20,000 of them; pensions were cut to ~60% before a 2016 top-up to >90% in Ontario — and whether the IP could have stayed. The enduring answer: with no domestic buyer of last resort, distressed strategic IP leaves the country.
No founder to say no, and no domestic bidder for the patents — the two gaps that define the leakage agenda.
