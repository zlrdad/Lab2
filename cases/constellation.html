01 The capital journey

A capital-allocation story, not a financing one.

Constellation raised almost no equity. Ontario pension capital seeded it in 1995; the 2006 IPO was a liquidity event for early holders, not a fundraise; growth came from the cash the acquired businesses threw off.

Feb 1995 · Launch
Founding backing ~C$25M
OMERS led the ~C$25M launch financing (secured through Mark Leonard's business-school network) — Ontario pension capital seeding a homegrown compounder.
Feb 2001 · Private placement
Placement ~C$60M
A ~C$60M placement alongside TD Capital. Thereafter, acquisitions were funded primarily from internally generated cash flow.
May 2006 · TSX IPO (CSU)
IPO ~C$80M
~C$17/share · ~US$165M revenue · ~45 businesses at IPO
Entirely secondary — a liquidity event for early holders including TD Capital, with zero primary capital to the company. Share count ~21.2M, roughly unchanged to this day.
2021 · 2023 · TSXV spin-outs
Topicus (TOI) · Lumine (LMN)
The European VMS arm (Topicus, 2021) and the communications/media arm (Lumine, 2023) were spun out to the TSX Venture Exchange as dividends-in-kind — two new controlled compounders on a domestic venture board.
2026 · Compounding at home
Market cap ~C$62B
Never dual-listed in the US, never relocated, never dependent on US venture capital. Leonard steps down from the board after the May 2026 AGM; COO Mark Miller now President.

02 The defining moment
Self-funding the roll-up — capital allocation as the moat.
Constellation's defining move was structural, not a single deal: acquire vertical-market software at high returns on invested capital through six decentralized operating groups (Volaris, Harris, Topicus, Vela, Jonas, Perseus), retain the cash, and redeploy it into the next cohort. In 2025 it deployed over €700M into acquisitions. Discipline is reinforced by long-horizon governance — no stock split ever, no quarterly earnings calls since 2018, communication almost entirely through Leonard's annual shareholder letters.
Why it mattered: because growth was self-funded, there was no recurring dependence on equity markets — the vulnerability that pulls Canadian scale-ups toward US capital and, eventually, US ownership. It is the proof that the TSX can host multi-decade technology compounding — with the catch that the model works because Constellation generates the cash, so it is not a template for pre-profit scale-ups.

03 The policy lens

What transfers, and what doesn't.

Constellation is the inverse of every leakage case in the vault — so the lens asks which parts of the model policy can actually seed, and which depend on a business few companies have.

Domestic institutional seed capital replicable
OMERS's ~C$25M 1995 backing catalysed a homegrown public champion — the direct case for Lever ① Supply (pension/patient capital seeding domestic companies).
A listing that isn't a way-station replicable
A senior-exchange listing need not lead to a US listing or a sale. Index inclusion and domestic ownership are policy-adjacent — Lever ④ Liquidity & coverage.
Long-horizon governance partly replicable
No split, no quarterly calls since 2018, annual letters. The culture is idiosyncratic, but listing-rule flexibility that permits low-frequency, long-horizon disclosure maps to Lever ③ Retention.
The self-funding moat can't be legislated
High-ROIC, cash-generative acquisitions are a business model, not a policy. It is exactly what the capital-hungry cohort in the financing-gap debate lacks.
LEVER ① + ③ · SUPPLY & RETENTION
Seed it, then keep it
Domestic institutional capital at the seed stage, plus listing rules that let a champion compound at home for decades.
04 Replicability verdict

Retention proven — but on a model few can copy.

Stayed
The transferable lessons are the listing and the seed capital — not the moat.
Two things generalise: a senior-exchange listing need not be a stepping stone to a US listing or a sale, and domestic institutional capital at the seed stage matters (OMERS, 1995). The capital-allocation moat depends on a cash-generative business, so it doesn't help the pre-profit companies the gap debate most worries about. Succession under Mark Miller — Leonard leaves the board after the May 2026 AGM — is the open question hanging over the thesis.
Constellation proves the TSX can compound a world-class company for two decades. It just hasn't done it twice.
