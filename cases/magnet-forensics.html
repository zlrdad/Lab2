01 The capital-markets journey

Revenue-funded to the TSX — then off it in under two years.

Magnet was unusually lightly venture-funded for its scale: largely revenue-funded, no pre-IPO mega-rounds. It reached the public markets, performed well, and then left them for US private ownership.

2011 · Founded · Waterloo
Revenue-funded
Founded by Jad Saliba (ex-Waterloo Regional Police, high-tech crimes unit) and Adam Belsher (ex-RIM/BlackBerry, CEO). Built digital-forensics software (Magnet AXIOM); grew largely on revenue, not venture mega-rounds.
Apr 28 2021 · TSX IPO (MAGT)
Raise ~C$115M
priced C$17.00 (~C$100.1M base); opened C$22.50 (+32%); ~C$115M gross after upsizing
The first Waterloo-based IPO in 15 years (since Sandvine, 2006). Led by ex-BlackBerry alumni; chairman Jim Balsillie.

ARR C$92.0M

Healthy and profitable — not a distressed seller. A clean test of whether the TSX can retain a growth-tech issuer.
Jan 20 2023 · Take-private announced
Deal ~C$1.8B
~US$1.3B; C$44.25 cash/share (public); ~15.4% premium to prior close, ~41% to 90-day VWAP

02 The defining moment
The public-market exit — healthy, and gone in under two years.
Magnet was not a distressed seller: at its last full year public (FY2022) it had ~C$92M ARR growing ~50%, positive Adjusted EBITDA and ~C$147M of cash. A company that ended a 15-year Waterloo IPO drought spent under 24 months as a public company before being absorbed into foreign private ownership — a US PE consolidator, not a Canadian acquirer or the public market itself. This is the governance inversion of Shopify: Magnet's insiders (Belsher, Saliba and chairman Balsillie held all multiple voting shares and >95% of the voting power) used the same dual-class control not to resist a sale but to deliver the company to Thoma Bravo — taking C$39.00/share and rolling equity, while public holders received C$44.25 in cash.
Why it mattered: even when a Canadian company succeeds in reaching the public markets, the listing can prove short-lived. For the TSX the issue is durability: winning listings but losing them to take-privates within a couple of years does not build the deep roster of seasoned domestic issuers that anchors index inclusion, coverage and a self-reinforcing pipeline. And dual-class control is a retention mechanism only if the controllers want to retain.

03 The policy lens

Making the TSX a more durable home.

A premium take-private of a profitable company is a legitimate value-realisation event — the goal is not to block such deals but to ensure the public-market alternative is attractive enough that strong companies aren't structurally pushed off the exchange.

The public-company cost/benefit gap durability lever
Take-privates are partly driven by compliance burden, thin coverage and limited liquidity on a small-cap listing. Proportionate continuous-disclosure relief and scaled reporting change the calculus — Lever ④ · listing & disclosure.
Thin domestic institutional demand the discount problem
Many TSX tech small-caps trade at a discount with limited coverage, which makes a PE premium hard to refuse. Mobilizing Canadian pension/institutional capital into growth-stage public equities tightens that discount — Lever ① Supply.
Secondary-market liquidity & coverage the STAR-segment fix
Designated market-makers and mandated analyst coverage for qualifying mid-caps (Italy's STAR) make public valuations competitive with private bids — the cleanest causal liquidity fix, Lever ④.
Dual-class cuts both ways the nuance
The same founder-control structure that kept Shopify independent delivered Magnet into foreign ownership. Governance design is a retention lever only alongside aligned incentives to stay listed.
LEVER ① + ④ · SUPPLY & LIQUIDITY
A public listing worth keeping
Lower the cost of being a small public company, deepen the domestic bid, and add coverage/market-making so a strong issuer isn't structurally pushed off the TSX.
04 Replicability verdict

The TSX can win a listing and still lose the issuer.

Acquired
A healthy TSX issuer, absorbed into US private ownership within two years.
Magnet is a clean public-market-shrinkage case: not a failure, but a profitable, fast-growing issuer that left the exchange for a US-PE consolidator barely two years after listing — the export-of-ownership pattern arriving at the public-company stage rather than the venture stage. Operations stayed in Waterloo; ownership, control, IP and the listing left. The levers are about durability — lowering the cost of being a small public company, deepening the domestic bid, and adding coverage and liquidity so the public-market alternative can compete with a private premium.
Magnet ended a 15-year IPO drought — and left the exchange in under two years.
