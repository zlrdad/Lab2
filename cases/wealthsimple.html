01 The capital journey

A Canadian conglomerate anchored it early — and never let go.

Where most Canadian scale-ups import US venture capital and drift offshore, Wealthsimple was seeded, scaled and majority-owned by the Power Corporation group — a domestic financial conglomerate with the balance sheet and the strategic interest to keep the asset Canadian.

Sept 2014 · Founded · Toronto
Seed C$1.9M
Founded by Michael Katchen (CEO), Brett Huneycutt and Rudy Adler as a robo-advisor. A December 2015 merger with order-execution broker Canadian ShareOwner Investments gave it a registered-dealer backbone to build a full consumer-finance stack.
Apr 2015 · Power Financial enters
Series A C$10M → C$30M
Power Financial invested C$10M (expandable to a C$30M Series A total), then steadily increased exposure through three vehicles — Power Financial, IGM Financial and Portag3 Ventures. By 2019 the group's stake reached ~89% (a historical figure since diluted).
2021 · Growth round
Valuation ~C$5B
A 2021 round valued Wealthsimple near C$5B at the rate-and-flows peak, before a broader fintech re-rating. Power's control persisted through the cycle.
Oct 2025 · C$750M round
Raise up to C$750M
~C$10B post-money · C$550M primary + up to C$200M secondary
Co-led by Dragoneer and GIC, with CPP Investments a new investor and Power Corporation, IGM, ICONIQ, Greylock and Meritech participating. Power marked the carrying value of its direct stake up to roughly C$1.5B.
2026 · Private & domestically controlled
Valuation ~C$10B
~3M clients, C$100B+ AUA, profitable in 2024 and 2025. Power-group ownership ~52–55% equity (~59% voting / ~44% fully diluted, 2024–25 bases). No IPO announced, and no venue confirmed.

02 The defining moment
The domestic-control test — will a champion list at home?
In May 2026 Wealthsimple launched retail IPO access — letting its clients request shares in select Canadian and US IPOs at the offering price. The irony is exact: the product is about other companies' listings, not its own. Wealthsimple is the strongest domestic-control case among Canada's champions — a ~C$10B, profitable, Power-Corporation-majority-owned fintech — which makes it a near-ideal natural experiment. If even a company this Canadian, this large, and this well-capitalised chooses a US listing, the structural pull of US markets is decisive. If it lists on the TSX, it validates the case that Canadian public markets can host a marquee tech IPO.
Why it mattered: as of mid-2026 there is no announced IPO and no confirmed venue — any "TSX candidate" framing is analyst inference, not company guidance. But the secondary component of the 2025 round, the profitability, and the scale all point toward eventual liquidity. Wealthsimple is the live test of whether Ontario can retain not just a head office but a listing — and the decision rests largely with Power's strategy, not the founders alone.

03 The policy lens

A retention advantage — if the exchange earns the listing.

Wealthsimple starts from a position most champions never reach: domestic majority control. The policy question is narrower and sharper — whether the TSX can be a competitive-enough venue that a Canadian-controlled champion has no reason to list abroad.

Domestic conglomerate control the retention head start
Power's majority stake means an IPO decision — and a listing venue — rests with a Canadian owner with a strategic interest in keeping the asset home. The rare structural advantage other champions lack; a demand-side outcome policy did not have to manufacture.
TSX competitiveness for a marquee float the decisive lever
The listing will go where liquidity, index inclusion, coverage and valuation are best. Making the TSX competitive for a large tech IPO — depth, comparables, institutional bid — is Lever ④ · liquidity & listing.
Domestic institutional bid at scale who anchors the book
A C$10B float needs a deep domestic institutional order book to price at home. Mobilizing Canadian pension and institutional demand into large growth-stage equities is Lever ① · supply — the same bid that would tighten the small-cap discount.
Concentration & valuation durability the nuance
Single-conglomerate control is retention-positive but concentrates the outcome; the C$10B mark reflects a strong rate-and-flows environment. Neither is a policy lever — but both temper the confidence of the "stay home" read.
LEVER ① + ④ · SUPPLY & LISTING
An exchange worth listing on
Deepen the domestic institutional bid and make the TSX competitive for a marquee float, so a Canadian-controlled champion has every reason to stay — and list — at home.
04 Replicability verdict

The best retention case Canada has — and an unfinished one.

Stayed private · domestic
Domestic control kept ownership home; the listing decision is the open question.
Wealthsimple is the strongest counter-example to the flight-to-the-US pattern: a scaled, profitable champion whose ownership and control are genuinely Canadian, held by a conglomerate with a reason to keep it that way. But the retention is only half-proven — the ownership stayed home; the listing has not been tested. The replicable lesson is that domestic control is the surest retention mechanism there is; the unfinished task is a Canadian public market competitive enough that even this champion has no reason to float abroad.
Ownership stayed Canadian. Whether the listing does is the test Ontario still has to pass.
