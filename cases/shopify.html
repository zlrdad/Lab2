01 The capital journey

Ottawa to a dual listing — without a detour through the Valley.

Shopify raised ~US$122M privately, went straight to a senior dual listing, and thereafter funded itself with convertible debt rather than dilutive equity — a financing path that kept the Canadian listing and headquarters intact.

2010–2013 · Pre-IPO
Six private rounds ~US$122M
Bessemer led the Series A/B and, reportedly pressed by peers to relocate Shopify to Silicon Valley, backed Tobi Lütke in Ottawa instead — partly on the city's talent-cost advantage. OMERS Ventures and Insight led the US$100M Series C (Dec 2013).
May 2015 · TSX + NYSE IPO
IPO US$131M
~US$1.27B valuation · priced US$17, closed ~US$28 day one (+50%)
A simultaneous dual listing on the NYSE (SHOP) and TSX — never listed on the TSXV. RBC and BMO were among the Canadian co-managers, embedding Shopify in the domestic market rather than floating US-only.
2018–2021 · Post-IPO
Convertible senior notes US$600M · 920M · 2.15B
Capital raised through convertible debt, not secondary equity — deep US-market access while keeping the Canadian listing and HQ.
2026 · Independent & Canadian
Market cap ~C$202B
Still TSX/NYSE-listed, founder-controlled, headquartered in Canada — the centre of gravity shifted Ottawa → Toronto with Lütke's 2024 move. The #1 technology issuer on the TSX.

02 The defining moment
The Founder Share — engineering permanent control.
In June 2022, facing a "dilution sunset" that would automatically collapse its dual-class structure, Shopify created a novel Founder Share granting Lütke a variable number of votes equal to 40% of total voting power, paired with a 10-for-1 stock split. Three proxy advisers (ISS, Glass Lewis, Egan-Jones) recommended against; 53.68% of non-Lütke shareholders approved. The share sunsets if Lütke leaves or drops below 30% of his original Class B holdings.
Why it mattered: unlike a fixed 10:1 class that erodes as new shares are issued, the variable Founder Share is a permanent governance floor — it gives Lütke the votes to reject any acquisition, hostile bid, or relocation pressure. It is the single most replicable retention mechanism on the TSX, and the exact protection the leakage cases lacked.

03 The policy lens

Which retention factors are actually replicable.

Shopify is the positive case, so the lens inverts: of the things that kept it home, which are idiosyncratic — and which are levers Ontario can pull for the next champion?

Dual-class / Founder Share replicable — structural
A governance floor that lets a founder refuse a sale or move. The most transferable lesson — maps to Lever ③ Retention (listing-rule flexibility for durable founder control).
Dual TSX/NYSE listing replicable — structural
A domestic listing signals Canadian identity, brings index inclusion and domestic institutional ownership — Lever ④ Liquidity & coverage (TSX competitiveness).
Regulatory embeddedness replicable — for regulated products
Deep integration with CRA/tax and Canadian payments creates switching costs. Regulatory design that builds domestic switching costs is a demand-side lever.
Founder attachment · no strategic acquirer idiosyncratic — not a lever
Lütke's personal ties and the absence of a natural buyer cannot be designed. The eroding talent-cost edge is fading on its own.

Retention architecture
Governance, liquidity, conditionality and defence — the levers that turn Shopify's one-off retention into a repeatable option.
04 Replicability verdict

A genuine retention — but a proof of concept, not a template.

Stayed
Governance and regulatory embeddedness are replicable; the rest is luck.
Shopify shows a world-class company can stay Canadian-owned and TSX-listed. But the mechanisms that did it split cleanly: the Founder Share and regulatory embeddedness are structural and copyable; the founder's personal attachment, the absence of a natural acquirer, and an eroding talent-cost edge are not. The policy task is to manufacture the copyable half for the next company — because most Canadian champions never had the option to say no.
Founder control is what let Shopify say no. Most Canadian champions never had the option.
