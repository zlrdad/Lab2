01 The capital journey

Canadian institutions showed up early — and stayed followers.

Autonomous driving is among the most capital-intensive segments in technology. Waabi raised ~US$1.28B across three priced rounds, all US-led (Khosla, G2). Canadian Crown and pension capital (BDC, EDC, OMERS, BMO, TELUS) participated in every round — but never led.

02 The defining moment
The record round — kept in Toronto, led from abroad.
The January 2026 Series C — a US$750M equity round plus ~US$250M in milestone capital from Uber — was claimed as the largest-ever Canadian tech fundraise, and Waabi stayed headquartered in Toronto. Yet its two co-leads (Khosla, G2) are US funds, its largest financial cheque came from BlackRock, and its commercialization channels are foreign-controlled: robotaxis run exclusively on Uber's platform, and the truck is Volvo's. Canadian institutions — BDC, EDC, OMERS, BMO, TELUS — were on every round, but only ever as participants.
Why it mattered: Waabi is, so far, a retention success — a globally significant deep-tech company founded, headquartered and scaled in Toronto. But the retention is founder-dependent and not yet structurally locked in: the company is private, foreign capital leads its financing, and its value chain routes through foreign strategics. The decisive test is the next financing or exit.

03 The policy lens

Show up early, then lead the round.

Waabi's domestic institutions did the hard part — they backed a frontier deep-tech company at the earliest stage. The unmet step is leading, not following, and locking retention in structurally.

Returnee founder & talent magnet ecosystem-replicable
Urtasun built Waabi in Toronto, anchored to the U of T / Vector talent base. Sustained Vector / U of T funding and retention policy keeps the talent pipeline that originates such companies.
Domestic capital follows, never leads the core gap
BDC, EDC, OMERS, BMO and TELUS joined every round but ceded lead-investor role — and its pricing power — to US funds. Structures to lead large patient-capital rounds are Lever ① Supply.
No governance moat / undecided exit the Shopify gap
No publicly disclosed founder-control or dual-class structure; listing/exit path undecided. Founder-control governance and a TSX-inclusive path are Lever ③ Retention.
Foreign-controlled commercial channels beyond securities policy
Uber's robotaxi platform and Volvo's trucks route the value chain through foreign corporates and raise acquisition optionality. Any control transaction sits with federal investment review (ICA), not a provincial lever.
LEVER ① + ③ · SUPPLY & RETENTION
Lead the cheque, then lock the domicile
Domestic capital that anchors deep-tech rounds instead of following, plus founder-control governance and a TSX-inclusive path that make staying the rational choice.
04 Replicability verdict

A retention success — so far, and not yet locked in.

Stayed
Originated and kept at home — but the next round is the real test.
Waabi is a genuine win: a returnee founder built a globally competitive deep-tech company in Toronto and kept it there through ~US$1.28B of financing, with real Canadian institutional participation. But it is private, US-led, and channel-dependent on foreign strategics, with no disclosed governance moat. The replicable lessons are upstream — domestic capital that leads deep-tech rounds, and retention structures that survive the next financing or exit.
Canada originated and kept the company. It has not yet been the one leading the cheque.
