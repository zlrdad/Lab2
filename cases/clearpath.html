Clearpath Robotics

Acquired · healthy-company leakage
A healthy, world-leading Waterloo robotics company acquired by US industrial giant Rockwell Automation in 2023. The founders did well and stayed — but ownership, control and IP moved permanently to a US parent. The cleaner, harder form of value leakage.

01 The capital journey

Capital-efficient — and with US strategics on the cap table from the start.

Clearpath sold hardware to fund growth before raising institutional venture capital, and raised only C$100M+ across its major rounds — modest relative to the eventual exit. Two US industrial strategics on the early cap table foreshadowed the buyer.

2009 · Founded · Waterloo
UWaterloo spinout
Founded by four University of Waterloo mechatronics grads — Rendall, Gariepy, Martinson, Webb. Spun out the OTTO Motors AMR division (autonomous mobile robots for material handling) in 2015.

02 The defining moment
Leakage of a healthy company — the harder case.
Unlike a distressed sale, Clearpath sold from strength: a recognized global leader in industrial AMRs, a decade-plus of Waterloo-developed autonomy IP, the largest robotics exit in Canadian history, and founders who did well and stayed in their roles (Rendall CEO of OTTO; Gariepy VP of Robotics at Rockwell). Yet the policy outcome is leakage: ownership and control moved to a US parent, the long-run upside now accrues to Rockwell's US shareholders, and ~US$313M of intangibles — a decade of Waterloo IP — became a US-owned asset. The strategic-acquirer pattern was visible years ahead: GE and Caterpillar Ventures sat on the cap table from the Series A/B.
Why it mattered: this is the cleaner, harder-to-criticize form of leakage — and the more common one. Ontario cannot fault founders for a strong all-cash exit from a credible global buyer. The policy question is whether the menu of alternatives — a domestic acquirer, a viable TSX robotics IPO, patient domestic capital deep enough to stay independent — ever realistically existed. For Clearpath, it did not.

03 The policy lens

The levers are upstream of the sale.

Nothing after the deal is an OSC matter — the leakage is done. What could have changed the outcome sits years earlier, in the depth of domestic capital and the credibility of a domestic exit path.

No domestic acquirer or public-market path the structural gap
For a deep-tech hardware leader whose natural buyers are foreign industrial incumbents, a US strategic exit is the path of least resistance. A credible TSX/TSXV path so independence is fundable is Lever ④ Liquidity.
Domestic growth capital, no domestic exit the yield problem
iNovia, Kensington, BMO, EDC and BDC funded the growth stage — but it produced a one-time cash return, not an enduring Canadian anchor. Deeper patient pools that can keep a company independent are Lever ① Supply.
Strategic acquirer visible years ahead a leading indicator
GE and Caterpillar Ventures on the Series A/B cap table pointed to the eventual buyer. Not a lever itself, but a signal of where retention risk concentrates in deep-tech hardware.
IP export post-sale no lever after close
~US$313M of Waterloo intangibles now capitalize on a US balance sheet — the exact mechanism in Value Capture & IP Loss. There is no securities lever after the deal; the levers are all upstream.
LEVER ① + ④ · SUPPLY & LIQUIDITY
Change the menu, not the decision
Domestic patient capital and a credible exchange path deep enough that a globally competitive Canadian robotics company can stay independent rather than default to a US strategic exit.
04 Replicability verdict

A good outcome that is still leakage.

Acquired
Founders enriched, jobs retained — Canadian ownership, control and upside lost.
Clearpath checks the "good story" boxes Element AI failed — founders enriched, jobs and HQ retained, IP-rich product still built in Waterloo. But on the dimensions value-retention policy actually targets — Canadian ownership, control, and durable public-market upside — it is a clean leakage outcome, and a more common one than a distressed sale. The replicable lessons are upstream of the deal: building domestic growth-stage pools and a credible exchange pathway deep enough to keep a champion independent and Canadian-owned.
The founders won. Canada lost a robotics champion anyway.
