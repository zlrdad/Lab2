01 The capital journey

Bootstrapped because it had to be — then a pension led the round.

Unable to raise conventional venture capital at the outset, StackAdapt's founders grew the company on revenue — an unusual, capital-efficient path to scale. When institutional capital finally arrived, the lead at the decisive stage was a Canadian pension, not a US fund.

2014 · Founded · Toronto
Bootstrapped
Founded by Ildar Shar, Vitaly Pecherskiy and Yang Han. Unable to raise conventional VC early, they built a programmatic advertising platform (a demand-side platform) on revenue — profitable and self-funding as it scaled.

~US$1B valuation · reportedly largely secondary
A previously undisclosed ~US$300M investment from US-based Summit Partners — reported as mostly secondary (liquidity to existing holders rather than fresh balance-sheet capital).

Vitaly Pecherskiy became CEO (Jan 1, 2024); Yang Han continues as CTO; Ildar Shar moved to the board — setting up the company's global-growth phase.

Led by Teachers' Venture Growth (TVG), the late-stage arm of the Ontario Teachers' Pension Plan — with Intrepid Growth Partners (ex-CPPIB CEO Mark Machin's new firm, its first investment) and four undisclosed investors.

02 The defining moment
A Maple-8 pension leads the round.
The February 2025 US$235M round led by Teachers' Venture Growth is the capital-mobilization thesis made concrete. The central argument of this report's supply-side work — that Canadian pension and institutional capital can and should anchor domestic growth companies, rather than letting that capital flow offshore or forcing the company to seek a US lead — is precisely the behaviour OTPP demonstrated here. A Maple-8 pension led a large growth round in a Canadian champion, keeping ownership and decision-making onshore, with a second Canadian investor (Intrepid) alongside.
Why it mattered: it proves the domestic bid exists when the company is profitable and de-risked. The caveat is honest — both the 2022 and 2025 rounds were largely secondary, recycling capital to existing holders more than fuelling the balance sheet, and OTPP entered a profitable ~US$2.5B company closer to a pre-IPO crossover than the earlier-stage gap the thesis most worries about. The behaviour is right; the open question is whether it generalises to riskier, earlier champions.

03 The policy lens

Do it earlier, and make it primary.

StackAdapt shows the domestic-capital lever working — a pension leading a champion's round. The unmet steps are pulling that capital earlier in the risk curve, and channelling it into primary growth funding rather than secondary liquidity.

Pension capital that leads the thesis, proven
TVG/OTPP leading — not following — a domestic champion's round is exactly Lever ① · supply: mobilizing Maple-8 and institutional capital into Canadian growth companies so ownership stays onshore.
Late-stage, low-risk entry the generalization gap
OTPP entered a profitable, ~US$2.5B company — near a pre-IPO crossover. The harder gap is earlier-stage, riskier champions. Structures that de-risk earlier institutional entry (co-investment, fund-of-funds) extend the lever down the curve.
Secondary vs. primary growth fuel, not just liquidity
Both rounds were mostly secondary — strong for retention, weaker as balance-sheet fuel. Directing mobilized capital toward primary growth financing is what turns a liquidity event into a scaling engine.
Exit venue the retention signal to come
A future IPO on the TSX vs. Nasdaq will be the decisive retention signal; many Canadian champions list in the US. A competitive domestic listing is Lever ④ · liquidity & listing.

04 Replicability verdict

The thesis in action — with one honest asterisk.

Stayed private · domestic
A Canadian pension led a champion's round — proof the domestic bid is real.
StackAdapt is the cleanest live evidence that Maple-8 capital will write large cheques into Canadian software when the company is profitable and de-risked — a direct counter to the "no domestic growth capital" narrative, with HQ, leadership and ownership anchored in Toronto. The asterisk is that the round was mostly secondary and entered late, so it validates the retention story more than the growth-funding one. The replicable lesson is the behaviour itself; the frontier is doing it earlier, and as primary capital.
A Maple-8 pension led the round. The proof of concept exists — the task is to make it routine, and make it earlier.
