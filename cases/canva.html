01 The capital journey

A capital desert at the start — a private colossus at the end.

Canva's journey captures the early-stage capital problem in its rawest form, and the other end of it too: a profitable, product-led company that used a controlled secondary market for liquidity instead of an IPO.

2013 · Founded · Perth / Sydney
Product-led
Founded by Melanie Perkins, Cliff Obrecht and Cameron Adams (the idea traces to Fusion Books, 2007) — a globally ambitious founder from Perth with, in her words, "zero Silicon Valley connections."
2010–2013 · The rejection years
Passed by ~100 VCs
Roughly 100 venture funds rejected Perkins over ~3 years; Australia's domestic early-stage market was too thin to back her. The breakthrough came through a personal network bridge (Bill Tai / Lars Rasmussen) to US investors — not a domestic institution.
2013 onward · Australian anchors
Flywheel capital
Earliest believers included Blackbird and AirTree — domestic funds seeded in part by the Atlassian generation's wealth; also Felicis and Sequoia. A prior champion's success created the local capital that backed the next.
2017 onward · Profitability
Profitable ~8 yrs
Reached profitability early and stayed profitable for ~8 years on a product-led model — extraordinary at multi-billion revenue, and the thing that gives it the option to stay private indefinitely.
2018–2025 · The valuation arc
US$1B → US$40B → ~US$26B
US$1B (2018) → US$40B (2021) → a ~US$26B markdown (2022–23) → back up. Growth funded largely without public markets.
Aug 2025 · Private at scale
Secondary at ~US$42B
A controlled employee/secondary sale at ~US$42B delivered liquidity to staff and early investors while keeping the company private and founder-controlled — raising no primary capital. ~US$4B ARR (+43% YoY). A 2026 IPO is widely speculated.

02 The defining moment
Secondaries instead of an IPO — decoupling liquidity from going public.
Rather than IPO, Canva used controlled secondary sales — the August 2025 employee sale at a ~US$42B valuation — to deliver liquidity to staff and early investors while staying private and founder-controlled. This is a structurally important model: it decouples employee liquidity from going public. Profitability is what makes it possible — Canva doesn't need public markets for capital, so it can stay private indefinitely.
Why it mattered for Canada: the absence of robust private secondary infrastructure in Canada is a real gap — it pushes companies toward either premature sale or a US listing to create liquidity. And upstream, Perkins' ~100 rejections are the Canadian seed-stage story too: strong founders, thin domestic risk capital, dependence on US networks to get going.

03 The policy lens

What Australia's ecosystem had that Canada's is missing.

Canva's two ends — the seed desert and the private-at-scale liquidity model — map onto two concrete Canadian gaps and one flywheel Canada most needs to engineer.

Private secondary-market infrastructure the private-at-scale model
Canva shows employee/founder liquidity can be delivered without an IPO or a sale. A deeper, better-regulated Canadian secondary market relieves the pressure that forces premature exits — Lever ④ · Growth & Venture Exchanges.
The seed desert is the first leak upstream of everything
The ~100-rejection story is where US-dependence starts. If domestic seed capital can't back globally ambitious founders, the leak begins at day one. Investor-side incentives target exactly this stage.
Flywheels compound Blackbird / AirTree
Atlassian-era wealth funding Blackbird and AirTree, which then backed Canva, is the founder-recycling mechanism Canada most needs to engineer deliberately — via capital-gains treatment and co-investment.
Private-at-scale has a cost too the honest limit
Staying private also limits retail and pension participation in the upside — the flip side of the IPO-decline problem, not a clean win. The lesson is optionality, not perpetual privacy.
LEVER ① + ④ · SEED & SECONDARIES
Back the founder, then give them a way to stay
Seed-stage incentives and a founder flywheel so domestic capital backs the next Perkins — plus secondary-market infrastructure so scaling doesn't force a sale or a US listing.
04 Transferability verdict

Private at scale — a model and a warning.

Comparator · Australia
Profitability buys freedom — and a secondary market delivers liquidity without a sale.
Canva is the middle-power champion that reached enormous scale without going public — proof that profitability plus a controlled private-secondary market can keep a company independent and founder-controlled indefinitely. For Canada the transferable lessons are concrete: build private secondary-market infrastructure so scaling doesn't force a premature exit; fix the seed desert that produced ~100 rejections; and engineer the founder flywheel that let Atlassian-era money back Canva. The honest caveat: private-at-scale also keeps the upside from retail and pension investors — the aim is optionality, not perpetual privacy.
Canva never needed the public market. That is both its freedom and Canada's problem.
