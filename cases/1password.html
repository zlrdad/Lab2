01 The capital journey

Fourteen years of no capital — then US$920M, all US-led.

1Password did not raise because it was short of cash — it was profitable throughout. It raised to accelerate a pivot into enterprise security. Every lead investor across all three rounds was American.

2005–2019 · Bootstrap era
Raised US$0
Founded in 2005 as AgileBits by Dave Teare and Roustem Karimov; profitable and free-cash-flow positive, funding growth from operating cash flow for ~14 years — no outside capital.
Nov 2019 · Series A
Raise US$200M
Accel (lead) — reported the biggest single investment in Accel's then-35-year history, and 1Password's first outside money after 14 profitable years. "The secret is out."
Jul 2021 · Series B
Raise US$100M
US$2.0B valuation
Accel (lead again); Sound Ventures, Skip Capital; tech-founder angels (Lütke, Finkelstein, Butterfield, Cannon-Brookes, Farquhar).
Jan 2022 · Series C
Raise US$620M
US$6.8B valuation · largest Canadian round at the time (since surpassed by Waabi)
ICONIQ Growth (lead); Tiger Global, Lightspeed, Accel; Canadian participation via Backbone Angels and founder-angels — not domestic institutional funds.
2025–26 · Private, Toronto-HQ'd
ARR >US$400M
~180,000 business customers; >75% of revenue from business; FCF-positive. CEO David Faugno: "at the scale and profitability that we could be public today" — but weighing, not rushing, an IPO (2026–27).

02 The defining moment
"We could be public today" — and choosing not to be.
1Password is the rare profitable, low-burn, marquee Canadian company — arguably the best case for domestic capital — and it still raised every lead cheque abroad, because no Canadian fund routinely leads a US$200M+ growth round. Then, unlike Shopify or Lightspeed, it took the private-growth path: CEO David Faugno has said the company is at the scale and profitability that it "could be public today," yet is not "rushing out to go public," weighing a possible IPO in 2026 or 2027.
Why it mattered: this is the textbook IPO-decline dynamic — a company that easily could be public choosing to stay private because abundant late-stage private capital removes the pressure — keeping price discovery, liquidity and index inclusion out of Canadian public markets. The pending IPO-venue decision is the live retention question.

03 The policy lens

If the best case still raised abroad, the gap is structural.

1Password's retention rests on profitability and the absence of a natural acquirer, not on domestic capital ties or governance entrenchment. Two levers matter, and one decision is still open.

Profitability-driven independence the healthiest base
Free-cash-flow positive removes forced-sale and down-round pressure — the strongest, least-fragile retention footing on the roster, though not itself a policy lever.
All three rounds US-led the structural gap
No Canadian fund leads US$200M+ growth rounds; even a profitable, low-risk marquee company defaulted to US leads. Domestic growth-capital depth is Lever ① Supply.
IPO venue undecided the live retention question
A US-only IPO would deepen the foreign anchoring already in the cap table; a TSX-inclusive dual listing would embed Canadian institutional holders — Lever ④ Liquidity & TSX competitiveness.
No domestic public anchor while private the IPO-decline cost
Staying private keeps a scaled Canadian champion off the TSX and out of Canadian pension/retail reach. Deeper private-secondary infrastructure and a competitive listing on-ramp reduce the default-to-private pull.
LEVER ① + ④ · SUPPLY & LIQUIDITY
Depth at growth stage, and a listing worth choosing
Domestic capital that can lead US$200M+ rounds, plus a TSX on-ramp attractive enough that the next 1Password lists at home.
04 Replicability verdict

The best case for domestic capital — and it still raised abroad.

Stayed
A stay built on profitability — not on domestic capital or a domestic listing.
1Password has stayed Canadian on the strength of profitability and the absence of a natural acquirer — the healthiest possible retention base. But it is also a clean diagnosis of the gap: the profitable, low-risk, best-case Canadian company still raised every lead cheque in the US, and remains private with no TSX listing. The retention risk is the IPO venue: a US-only listing would harden the foreign anchoring, where a TSX-inclusive one would build the Canadian capital-market connective tissue Shopify has.
A profitable Toronto company that could be public tomorrow — and raised every dollar in the US.
