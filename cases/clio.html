01 The capital journey

Foreign leads at every stage — with one Canadian name.

Clio's capital story is the heart of the case: a Canadian champion built increasingly on foreign — overwhelmingly US — growth capital, because no domestic pool wrote cheques of US$250M–US$900M at the relevant stages. The only Canadian institutional name on the whole cap table is OMERS.

02 The defining moment
The US$900M Series F — scale, bought with US capital.
The 2024 Series F — US$900M at a US$3B valuation, led by US-based NEA — was the largest software financing in Canadian tech history. It contained "a substantial amount of secondary": much of the money bought out early employees and investors rather than funding the company. It is the textbook case of a Canadian company whose equity ownership and board influence migrate to US capital as it scales — even while HQ, executive team and the majority of staff stay in Canada. Control and economics tilt foreign; physical footprint and talent stay domestic.
Why it mattered: Clio is the cleanest test of the scale-vs-retain distinction. Scale was achieved — but on imported growth capital, because no Canadian pool wrote cheques at that size and stage. Retention is partial: unlike the acquired-and-absorbed cases, Clio was not hollowed out, but the financial value capture — equity appreciation, secondary proceeds, eventual IPO upside — accrues disproportionately to US investors. Retention of operations and retention of ownership are diverging.

03 The policy lens

Keep the ownership, not just the office.

Clio is a case where a domestic growth-capital vehicle — or a pension allocation — might have retained more Canadian ownership of a clear winner. The levers are about closing the growth-capital gap and making the domestic exit competitive.

The domestic growth-capital gap the core lever
No Canadian pool wrote the US$250M–US$900M cheques Clio needed, so US funds led every growth stage. Mobilizing domestic pension/institutional capital into scale-stage rounds is Lever ① · supply — directly testable against this case.
Ownership vs. operations retention the value-capture problem
Canada keeps the jobs and IP; the US keeps the equity upside. Retention policy has to target ownership, not just the head office — the value-capture distinction this report draws throughout.
Likely US IPO venue the exit signal
A US-dominated cap table, US-denominated metrics and US investor base point to a Nasdaq/NYSE listing (inferred, not confirmed). A competitive TSX for a scaled float is Lever ④ · liquidity & listing.
Deep private markets delay the forcing function the IPO-decline lens
A US$5B company can stay private indefinitely, fund M&A (vLex) and give liquidity via secondaries — removing the historical pressure to reach a public exchange at all. Retention has to compete with that patience.
LEVER ① + ④ · SUPPLY & LISTING
Close the growth-capital gap
Domestic capital that can lead US$250M+ scale rounds, plus a competitive domestic exit — so a Canadian winner keeps its ownership, not just its office.
04 Replicability verdict

Scale achieved — ownership exported.

Independent · US capital
A global Canadian champion whose equity upside migrated to US investors.
Clio proves a Canadian vertical-SaaS company can reach global scale — but it did so by importing US growth-stage equity, because the domestic capital simply was not there at the right cheque sizes. It has not been hollowed out: HQ, IP, executive team and most jobs remain in Canada. But its ownership and eventual upside tilt decisively foreign, and its likely exit is a US listing. It is the clearest illustration in the roster that retaining operations and retaining ownership are two different problems — and that the growth-capital gap is where Canada loses the second one.
Canada kept the company. The United States kept the upside.
