◇ Diagnosis I · public markets

Three boards. Three different worlds.

Canada lists companies on three exchanges — the senior TSX, the TSX Venture board, and the CSE. Up close they are not one market at different sizes; they are three different animals. Understanding the difference is the first step to seeing why the public-market pathway has thinned — and why the fix is not cheaper listings.

02 Liquidity is the binding constraint

The strongest single correlate of listings: turnover velocity.

Across a 79-exchange study, turnover velocity — annual value traded ÷ market cap — is the strongest single correlate of how many companies list (a leading explanation, not proven causation). On a single consistent basis (WFE value traded ÷ market cap, 2024) the Asian and US markets are in a different league, while Canada's boards sit in the lower-middle of the pack. The target is quality liquidity — deep institutional trading — not raw turnover: some of the leaders' velocity is retail churn Canada neither has nor should want to import wholesale.

The TSX is lower-middle of the pack on this consistent basis; the TSXV is thinner still — on a vault issuer-level computation it turns over at roughly ~45% (WFE does not report the junior board separately), with a severe tail: ~24% of names traded under C$100K over two months and ~10% didn't trade at all.
The Asian outliers run on retail: Korea ~64% and Taiwan ~54% of all turnover is retail order flow. Canada has neither that day-trading base nor a mandatory domestic-equity savings pool.

03 The IPO drought

The on-ramp to public markets has nearly closed.

New operating-company listings across all Canadian exchanges have fallen year after year. Part of that is the global cycle: 2021 was an anomalous worldwide IPO peak (ZIRP and the SPAC blow-off), and the freeze that followed hit every exchange — so the raw fall from a 2021 base overstates the Canadian-specific share. But the decline is not universal — Korea's listing count held roughly flat and Hong Kong is recovering, while Canada's has not. What is left after netting out the cycle is Canada-specific: a take-private drain that a near-dormant IPO pipeline no longer refills. The trickle that remains is mostly small and mostly mining.

Operating-company IPOs, all Canadian exchanges

2021 → 2025 · Private Capital Journal / CPE Analytics
2025: 19 IPOs raising C$1.54B — but only 3 were operating-company IPOs on the TSX (Private Capital Journal basis; the vault's TMX Issuers-List cut itemises 2 dated in calendar 2025 — see Sectors).
Beware the headline count: TMX records 257 "TSX IPOs" in 2025 — but almost all are CDRs and ETFs. Actual operating companies that IPO'd on the TSX: three. VC-backed IPO exits in 2024: zero.

And the base drains from the top faster than the on-ramp refills it. The thinning is a two-sided problem: while operating IPOs have fallen to a trickle, established issuers keep leaving through take-privates and acquisitions. Since 2023, roughly C$125B of companies have exited the TSX through privatisations, mergers and buyouts (a single-participant estimate — National Bank via BNN). Not every exit is a loss: a financial take-private is often a reversible recapitalisation that can re-list later, so the durable concern is the subset that is foreign strategic acquisition — control, IP and HQ moving offshore. In 2024 alone, privatisations were 56% of all private-equity dollars deployed in Canada — C$15.4B to take 14 companies private, the highest privatisation value on record — and Q3 2025 booked the most take-private deals of any quarter in three years. The named 2024–26 wave runs from Nuvei and Dayforce to Magnet Forensics (taken private by US-based Thoma Bravo), with Kinaxis and Dye & Durham circling. Delistings outrunning a near-dormant IPO pipeline is exactly how a senior board of ~672 operating issuers keeps shrinking.

source

And for the issuers who stay, the market barely does its core job — supplying equity capital. Raising equity is the whole point of a public market, yet total equity raised on the TSX and TSXV in 2024 was just C$21B — thin for a C$5.96T board. That equates to 0.4% of total market capitalisation, down from a 3.5% peak in 2009, though the ratio should be read as directional, not headline: it divides a single year's flow by the market-cap stock, so it falls mechanically as prices rise, 2009 is a crisis-year high (post-GFC bank recapitalisations), and the figure comes from a single think-tank report. The dollar amount carries the point. Meanwhile 37% of TSX Composite companies shrank their own share counts through buybacks over the prior three years — buybacks are normal capital return for mature, cash-generative firms, but co-occurring with a near-dead issuance pipeline they mean the net equity supply is shrinking, not just being recycled.

05 The missing middle

The barbell — and the gap where champions belong.

The TSX is a barbell: a cluster of mega-caps at one end, a long small-cap tail at the other, and a thin C$2–10B mid-cap waist in between. That waist is exactly where Canada's private champions would land if they listed at home.

06 The US exit ramp

US markets are one step away — access, and its pull.

The thin middle would matter less if Canada's champions had nowhere else to go. They do — the United States — and the door is unusually wide. 206 of the 672 operating TSX companies (30.7%) are already interlisted on a foreign exchange, up from 199 of 668 (29.8%) just three months earlier. Almost every one of those second listings is American: interlisting is a scale signal, not a flight — but it also means the on-ramp to a deeper market is already built, and it points one way.

About 85% of those second listings — 175 of 206 — are on a US venue (NYSE, Nasdaq or NYSE American). The venue-by-venue and sector-by-sector interlisting cut, and the 491 TSXV names (~36%) quoted on the US OTC market (Feb 2026), are on the interactive Exchanges page.

Why the door is so wide: the MJDS. For a seasoned issuer, a US listing is remarkably cheap because of the Multijurisdictional Disclosure System — a 1991 Canada–US mutual-recognition regime under which the SEC largely defers to Canadian review. An eligible company (a foreign private issuer, ≥12 months a Canadian reporting issuer in good standing, ≥US$75M public float) registers and reports in the US using its Canadian disclosure documents: a Form F-10 prospectus that is generally not SEC-reviewed — a deal can clear in days — and a 40-F annual report filed the same day as its Canadian AIF, with no US-GAAP reconciliation. MJDS strips out the duplication of being public in two countries.

What it does not strip out is Sarbanes-Oxley. §404 internal-control attestation still applies; the 2025 GAO review puts that at roughly US$700K a year for a single-location company, up to ~US$1.6M for a complex one. MJDS cuts the duplication cost, not the core governance cost of being a US reporting company.

Is the access worth it? The evidence is broadly positive: Doidge, Karolyi & Stulz find foreign firms cross-listed in the US trade at a Tobin's-q premium of about 16.5% — roughly 37% on a major US exchange — attributed to governance "bonding." But for Canadian issuers the premium is conditional, not automatic: King & Segal (Bank of Canada) show the uplift accrues only to firms that actually win US investor recognition and liquidity. A listing no one follows earns little durable re-rating — cross-listing is a platform, not a re-rating in itself.

MJDS is both the asset and the leak: frictionless access to the deepest capital pool on earth — with nothing on the Canadian side of the door to hold issuers home.

For companies too young for MJDS — pre-revenue deep-tech like Xanadu — the same US access runs through a far costlier SPAC merger (dilution instead of fees). Either way the ramp runs south and is smoothest for exactly the seasoned, scaled issuers the thin middle most needs to keep — a pull, not a one-way exodus: most interlisted names stay TSX-primary and Canadian-HQ'd, and interlisting is dual presence, not departure.

07 Who owns the market

A third is foreign; the domestic base is exposed but intermediated.

If the fix is a deeper domestic demand base, it helps to see who holds the market today. On the national ownership accounts, non-residents own a third of all Canadian listed equity (32.5%) — itself partly a sign of an open, liquid market global capital wants to hold — and Canadian households own only 16.5% directly. Look through the pensions and funds that hold shares on their behalf and households' ultimate stake rises to ~45.5%: households are heavily exposed, just indirectly. What is thin is the directly-held, actively-traded retail float — intermediated, largely passive capital does not turn a market over.

Ownership of Canadian listed equity — direct vs look-through

StatCan National Balance Sheet Accounts · Q1 2026 · Canada (national — no provincial split exists)
Two lenses on the same market. Direct: households 16.5% · foreign 32.5% · financial institutions 44.7% (of which trusteed pensions 13.6% and mutual funds 13.6%) · corporates + government 6.3%. Look-through reattributes pension, mutual, segregated and life-fund equity to the households who ultimately own it — lifting households to ~45.5% (it slightly overstates retail, since not all fund equity backs household claims). This household look-through is a different quantity from an institutional-ownership share, so it is not corroborated by the OECD's ~46% institutional-Canada figure — the numerical closeness is coincidence, not a cross-check. The ownership accounts are national only — no Ontario equity-ownership split exists. Whichever lens, the directly-held, actively-traded retail float is thin — the demand base the velocity gap has already flagged as missing.

08 The read

The constraint is quality and liquidity — not listing cost.

The junior boards are not short of listings; they are short of quality issuers and trading. Canada actually has the highest median free float in the OECD's growth-market study (82%) — the shares are available to trade, but under-traded and under-covered. The lever, then, is market-making and research coverage (and a retail / again-domestic demand base), not cheaper admission.

Which raises the next question: if the public on-ramp has narrowed, where is the capital that would scale these companies — and who is providing it?
