◇ Comparators · what works abroad

What the jurisdictions that kept their champions did.

Canada's problem — under-invested at the seed and scale-up stages (a conversion-and-retention gap more than a headline VC-quantity one; on the Compendium basis charted below Canada sits mid-pack), under-traded public markets, champions sold or scaled abroad — is not unique. Other middle powers faced the same gravity and built mechanisms to resist it: permanent fund-of-funds, contractual domestic reinvestment, retail tax wrappers, designated market-makers, mandatory savings pools. This is the comparator field, the working program toolkit, and the one model — Asian retail liquidity — that looks like magic but comes with an honest caveat.

02 The program toolkit

Eight mechanisms that actually moved capital.

Strip away the labels and the toolkit is small. Two families dominate: supply-side leverage (state co-investment that pulls in private capital, then exits) and demand-side anchoring (a standing domestic bid — pensions, retail, insurers). The cleanest causal liquidity fix is neither — it is market-making plus mandated coverage.

04 The Asian liquidity model

Retail turnover drives the velocity outliers — but read it carefully.

The exchanges with the highest turnover velocity run on retail order flow: Korea ~64% and Taiwan ~54% of all turnover is retail. They pair that demand base with a dedicated high-growth board and a graduation pipeline — KOSDAQ in Korea, the TSE Growth Market in Japan — so scaling companies have a venue with depth before they reach the senior board.

Turnover velocity, annualised

Value traded ÷ market cap · OECD Asia Capital Markets Report
Turnover velocity, annualised, on one consistent WFE 2024 basis (value traded EOB ÷ market cap). The US markets (NYSE ~96% / Nasdaq ~95%) sit above Canada's TSX (~64%); the Asian retail-driven exchanges lead. TSXV ~45% is a separate vault issuer-level computation — WFE does not report the junior board.
The retail→liquidity link is correlational, not proven causation. The cleanest causal lever is Italy's STAR — market-making plus mandated coverage. And Japan's NISA shows a retail wrapper can misfire: much of its flow went into foreign index funds, financing other countries' markets, not Japan's.

The lesson is not "import day-trading." It is: build a domestic demand base (a retail wrapper or mandatory savings), give scaling companies a real growth board, and guarantee liquidity at the security level through market-making and coverage. A wrapper that funds foreign funds (NISA) buys you nothing at home.

Four numbers behind the gap.

The mechanisms above are the supply side of the story. Four data points from the vault's International Comparator Data Compendium show the underlying position they are meant to correct: Canada builds few $1B companies per head, starves its angel stage, keeps its pension giants out of domestic venture, and offers investors no innovation-specific exit relief. Each is a lever the recommendations pull.

Unicorns per million people

Current-HQ basis · ~2026 · Compendium §6.1
Canada produces roughly 0.8 unicorns per million people — below Israel (~2.6), the US (2.4), and the small-economy leaders Singapore (5.8) and Sweden (4.6). It builds world-class companies; it builds few of them per head, and keeps fewer still (see the Champions page). Core-set rows are on the committed current-HQ basis; expansion comparators (Estonia ~7.1 ⚠️, etc.) are held out as not-yet-reconciled.
source

Tax-incentivised angel capital, per year

Log-scale gap · Compendium §7.1
Canada is the only G7 country with no angel-specific tax incentive. Its formal angel-group capital (~C$146M) sits against the UK's ~£1.8B EIS/SEIS across all channels and the US's ~US$18B — the UK deploys about 56× more tax-incentivised angel capital per year, or ~33× per capita. Bars are drawn on a log scale so all three are visible; the true gap is far wider than the pixels. The UK's 30–50% front-end relief and the US's QSBS exit relief are the mechanisms Canada lacks.
source

Pension allocation to venture / PE

Share of pension assets · Compendium §8.1
US public pensions put ~10.4% into PE incl. VC; European funds ~0.12% into VC — an ~87× gap. Canada's paradox is different: CPP (C$714B), CDPQ (~C$517B) and OTPP (~C$279B) are world-class global PE investors, yet deploy minimal domestic early- and growth-stage venture relative to that AUM. The standing domestic bid the toolkit's demand-side anchors create (Australia's super, Korea's NPS) is the thing Canada's giants don't yet provide at home.
source

Innovation-specific exit relief

Who rewards holding the winner · Compendium §10.2
US · QSBS 100% exclusion (5-yr) UK · EIS/SEIS 100% CGT exemption Australia · ESIC 10-yr exemption Sweden · ISK wrapper (~0.9%/yr) Singapore / Taiwan · 0% CGT Canada · none — LCGE is general; Entrepreneurs' Incentive eliminated
Headline rates barely tell the story. Canada's effective CGT (~13–27%) is competitive in isolation — but every retention leader pairs a targeted zero-CGT for backing and holding innovation: QSBS (US), EIS/SEIS (UK), ESIC (Australia) — while Sweden's ISK is a broad flat-tax wrapper, not an innovation-specific relief. Canada has no innovation-specific exit relief: the LCGE is general, the 2024 inclusion-rate hike was cancelled (Mar 21, 2025), and the Canadian Entrepreneurs' Incentive was eliminated. Nothing rewards an investor for holding the winner to scale at home.
source

Read together, the four are one position: too few champions per head, an under-funded angel stage, pension giants pointed abroad, and no tax reason to hold the winner. Every recommendation that follows targets one of these — which is why the comparator mechanisms above matter. These four are a preview: the full fourteen-economy comparator set — VC/GDP, market-cap share, R&D intensity, innovation rank, tax and market size — is laid out on The Comparators →.

05 The read

The fixes exist. None is a single switch.

No comparator solved retention with one instrument. The ones that worked stacked a supply-side leverage program, a domestic demand anchor, and security-level liquidity support — and, where the value mattered most, made domestic reinvestment contractual rather than voluntary. That stack is the raw material for the recommendations.
