◇ Chapter 2 · what Ontario already has

The raw materials are already here.

The last chapter set the stakes. This one answers the obvious next question — is the fight even winnable? It is, and the reason is that Ontario is not starting from nothing. It has world-class talent, a real economy, firm clean power, one of the deepest pools of institutional capital on earth, and champions that already work. Which is precisely why the failure to finance and retain its growth companies is so expensive: the endowment is strong; the conversion is broken. Every strength below is also a measure of what's being left on the table.
~10%
of the world's top-tier AI researchers · 2nd per capita on Earth
Canada hosts roughly a tenth of the planet's elite AI research talent — yet captures under 2% of global AI venture capital. The talent is world-class; the capital that should follow it is not. The gap between those two numbers is the whole report in miniature.

01 Talent & research density

A research base that punches far above the country's size.

Ontario's single deepest asset is people. The Toronto–Waterloo corridor is the highest-ranked startup ecosystem in Canada and a genuine global node — anchored by foundational AI research (Hinton at U of T), a world-leading quantum cluster (IQC, Perimeter), and the University of Waterloo's engineering-and-co-op machine. The talent is not the problem. What happens to it downstream is.

02 Institutional & market scale

This is a real economy — not a minnow.

Ontario's output is on the order of a mid-sized OECD member state, with a mature financial sector that has quietly become its largest. The scale is genuine — which is what makes the under-capitalisation of its growth companies a policy failure rather than an inevitability of size.

03 Firm, clean, competitively-priced power

The most underweighted advantage of the AI era.

The 21st century's most capital-intensive sectors — AI data centres, EV and battery plants, electrified manufacturing — compete for one scarce thing: firm, clean electricity. Ontario has it. A large nuclear fleet plus abundant hydro give it roughly three-quarters of generation as low-emitting, dispatchable baseload — firm clean power, not just intermittent renewables. The IMF singled this out as a competitive advantage for Canada in 2026.

Stated honestly. Ontario's power is competitive, not the cheapest in North America — Quebec (~4¢/kWh), Manitoba and BC undercut it on headline industrial price. And the advantage is contingent: IESO projects demand rising ~75% by 2050, and data-centre load is straining grids everywhere. The edge is the combination — firm clean power + competitive price + US-market proximity — and it holds only if generation is built ahead of the curve.

04 Deep capital — sitting latent

One of the deepest capital pools on Earth — pointed almost anywhere but home.

Canada's eight largest pension managers — the "Maple 8" — run roughly C$2.63 trillion combined and are among the most operationally sophisticated institutional investors in the world. The Big Six banks hold C$7T+ in assets on top of that. This is not a shortage of capital. It is a shortage of capital pointed at Ontario's own growth companies: under half a percent of Maple-8 assets reaches domestic early-stage venture. That is what makes it a latent strength — the single largest underleveraged asset in the whole diagnosis.

05 Sector breadth & proven champions

The bench is broad — and some of it already scaled and stayed.

Ontario is not a one-sector bet. It anchors Canada's AI and quantum economy, keeps a large advanced-manufacturing base, and hosts a growing fintech and life-sciences cluster. And the retention thesis is not hypothetical: companies have been built here, scaled, and kept — proof the model can work when the capital and listing conditions line up.

That is §04's latent pension capital working in practice — the proof the hinge can turn. Strength and risk sit in the same list; so does the fix.

06 A second cluster · Ottawa & Kanata North

A second national tech cluster — built, tellingly, on a champion's collapse.

Ontario's innovation base is not only the Toronto–Waterloo corridor. Two hours east, Kanata North bills itself as Canada's largest technology park — on the order of 800 firms and ~C$17.9B in annual economic output — anchored by Shopify's global headquarters, one of the deepest optical-networking and semiconductor R&D bases on the continent, and unusual proximity to the federal defence and security establishment. It is a genuine second node. It is also the whole report in one city: the cluster's world-class photonics density exists because Nortel — once Canada's most valuable company — collapsed in 2009 and scattered its engineers, and its modern winners keep getting acquired out. The talent stayed; the ownership too often did not.

A second real cluster proves again the raw materials are here — whether Ottawa keeps its next champion is the same open question as the rest of the report.

07 Where the strengths stop

None of this closes the gap on its own.

Honesty is the point of this page. The same ecosystem that scores 10 out of 10 on STEM talent scores 2 out of 10 on funding and 2 out of 10 on scaling experience — in the same benchmarking report. The assets are real; they run into a small home market, a slow adoption culture, and a missing layer of scale-up capital. The strengths get companies started. They do not get them scaled or kept. That handoff is the rest of the report.

08 From the endowment to the failure

Strong ingredients, broken conversion.

This chapter answered the winnability question: the raw materials are here, at world-class quality. Which reframes the whole problem — Ontario is not poor in talent, capital, energy or companies. It is poor at converting them into scaled, retained, publicly-listed champions. The chapters that follow are the conversion failure in detail — and then what to do about it.

The endowment is strong; the machine that turns it into champions is what's broken.
