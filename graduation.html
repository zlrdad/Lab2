The escalator.

A junior board only matters if companies can ride it up to the senior one. Canada has that escalator — the TSX Venture board feeds the TSX — and mechanically it works: 1 in 3 operating companies on the senior board began on the junior one. But almost all of them are mining, and the machine is idling. This page is the evidence base for the report's graduation-escalator lever.

01 The Canadian escalator

One board is built to feed the other.

The TSX Venture Exchange is designed as a stepping-stone: a company lists small and cheap on the junior board, scales, and graduates up to the senior TSX. An informal C$100M market-cap level is the working graduation-candidate threshold, and TMX's November 2025 reforms made the senior board's Original Listing Requirements more industry-agnostic. Underneath it all sits the Capital Pool Company on-ramp (§03). The stock figures — not any clean annual flow series, which does not exist publicly — carry this page.

02 Who rides the escalator

Forty-three percent of everyone who rides it is a miner.

The escalator inherits the venture board's tilt. Of the 222 graduates, 95 (43%) are mining, at a median C$1,648M. Only 19 technology and 21 life-sciences companies have ever graduated — and they cluster right at the threshold (median C$171M and C$141M), a fraction of mining's scale. The growth sectors the rest of this report is about are the thinnest bars on the chart.

03 The feeder & the pace

A uniquely Canadian on-ramp — and a stalling engine.

Under the escalator sits the Capital Pool Company program, a made-in-Canada shell-first on-ramp: raise a blind pool, list it, then complete a Qualifying Transaction into a real business. It works as a funnel — roughly a third of all TSX graduates began life as a CPC — but the vehicles are mostly tiny and thinly traded. And the escalator's pace has collapsed: there is no clean public annual graduation series, but every anchor points the same way.

04 Three escalators

The same machine, three different outcomes.

Every senior board needs a feeder, and three of them are instructive side by side. Graduation itself is the goal — a company moving up is a success — so the useful question is never "too much graduation," it is whether the junior board stays worth listing on. Sweden's First North → Nasdaq Stockholm is the escalator that works: heavy graduation and a feeder still healthy enough to keep pulling in new listings. Korea's KOSDAQ → KOSPI is the warning: its best names transfer up to escape a persistent junior-board valuation discount, and because no mature anchor ever stays, the venue never sheds it — a self-reinforcing loop. Canada's runs mechanically but barely carries the innovation sectors at all. The lesson is not "graduate less" — it is a junior board attractive enough that graduating is a milestone, not an escape hatch.

KOSDAQ's best names — Celltrion (2018), Alteogen and Ecopro BM (2025, ≈9% of its cap) — transfer up to escape a persistent valuation discount and institutional neglect; with no mature anchor left to stay, the venue can't shed it — "KOSPI's second league."
The design lesson, stated for Canada. Graduation is the goal, so the failure modes are not "too much" of it — they are different: Sweden's feeder stays healthy and keeps attracting new listings (~45% of new Swedish regulated-market listings come off a growth market); Korea's junior board becomes an escape hatch from its own discount; Canada's escalator barely runs for the innovation sectors at all. The aim is not fewer graduations — it is a junior board attractive enough that graduating is a milestone, not an escape. The TSXV sits on exactly this axis: its own liquidity discount, plus a US-OTC back door (491 venture issuers already trade OTC — see Exchanges), is the Korean loop's opening move; Sweden is the alternative to build toward.

05 The read

Fix what rides it; keep the junior board worth staying on.

This page is the evidence base for the report's graduation-escalator lever. The prescription is not to build an escalator — Canada has a functioning one — but to improve what rides it (TSXV liquidity, analyst coverage, and a growth-sector feeder) and to grade graduation quality, not listing count. The PISCES-style pre-IPO venue and the broader Growth & Venture Exchange question sit upstream of the escalator — they feed it, they do not compete with it. Sweden is the target; Korea is the guardrail.
