◇ Diagnosis II · the financing gap

Canada under-invests in its own companies.

If the public on-ramp has narrowed, the private one is where champions are actually built. But Canada deploys far less venture capital — relative to the size of its economy — than its peers, and the gap is sharpest exactly where it matters most: the scale-up rounds. What's missing gets supplied by US investors, who pull control and upside south.
~C$5–11B
per year · the venture-capital supply gap

01 The gap, normalised

06 The capital sitting idle next door

The money isn't absent — it's parked.

The institutional-allocation gap has a household-side mirror. Canadians hold a large pool of financial capacity that never reaches growth equity: roughly C$1.02 trillion of unused TFSA room nationally (about C$411B in Ontario), fewer than one in eleven holders maxed out, and a median RRSP contribution of just C$4,000. Most household wealth, meanwhile, sits in real estate rather than financial assets.

Unused registered-account room is a capacity measure, not idle cash — much unused room reflects limited disposable savings rather than a deployment choice (room accrues automatically regardless of income), so the mobilisable subset is far smaller than the C$1.02T ceiling and concentrated among higher-income households. But it quantifies the domestic financial pool that today sits outside the scale-up funnel — the household counterpart to the pension-allocation shortfall. TFSA / RRSP figures are national with Ontario shown where published; the real-estate tilt is from the 2023 Survey of Financial Security (Canada 47.5% · Ontario 51.2%).

The deficit, in other words, is not a shortage of Canadian savings — it is a mobilisation problem. Which is exactly where the lever sits.

source

And the household pool is only the near mirror. The larger reflection sits on institutional balance sheets, where the same story repeats at scale. Canadian non-financial corporations hold more than C$1.1 trillion in cash, and pension funds and asset managers together hold nearly US$10 trillion. Capital exists on every balance sheet — household room, corporate cash, pension pools — yet over 2015–24 more than C$1 trillion of investment net-exited the country, two dollars out for every dollar in.

Corporate cash and institutional pools are not "dead" money — firms hold liquidity for legitimate operating and precautionary reasons, and pension mandates require de-risked, long-duration assets Canada under-produces; like the household room above, these are capacity and allocation measures, not literally idle cash. But the scale is the point: only ~30% of Canadian capital formation flows into productivity-enhancing machinery/equipment and intellectual property — about half the US share, last in the G7. The benchmark for "mismatch" here is not "the US does more" but a market-failure one: scale-up formation carries positive externalities (spillover firms, IP, jobs) the private optimum under-supplies, so mobilising domestic capital is a coordination fix, not a demand that households mis-allocate. Canada's problem is a capital mismatch, not a capital shortage — the institutional mirror of the household mobilisation gap. The corporate-cash, institutional-pool and net-exodus figures are single-source (RBC Thought Leadership, Capital Gains, 2026 — Tier-4, read as directional); a StatCan National Balance Sheet Accounts cross-check would upgrade them.

One balance sheet completes the picture — the funds themselves. There is a standard objection to any financing-gap claim: isn't there record dry powder — capital already committed to funds, just waiting to be deployed? Globally, yes. Private-equity dry powder reached roughly US$3.7 trillion at the start of 2026, about double its 2019 level. But that abundance sits at the buyout and late-stage end, in aging 2022–23 vintages — and almost none of it is Canadian. Canada's own venture dry powder is about C$11.5 billion, a fraction of one percent of the global pool, and it is both shrinking and re-allocating. The decisive number is composition: the share available for initial, new-company cheques fell from 81% (2022) to 42% (2025) — the lowest on record, with nearly two-thirds now reserved for follow-ons. Even the dry powder Canada has is retreating from new bets.

Dry powder is capital LPs have committed to VC/PE funds but managers have not yet called and deployed — the standard objection to a gap thesis. On the Canadian data it sharpens the thesis: the glut is real but concentrated in large-cap buyout / late-stage strategies and aging vintages, while Canada's pool is small, shrinking and re-allocating to follow-ons. Scope, honestly: no published dataset splits dry powder into listed-vehicle vs private-partnership buckets, and there is no clean uncommitted-capital series comparing public-sector funds (BDC / EDC / CIB) with private funds — neither split is claimed here. The one defensible public-vs-private reading is private-fund capital vs public-market capital formation: committed private capital sits at records while Canadian public-market equity formation has collapsed (zero VC-backed IPOs since 2023 — the Public Markets chapter carries that counterpart). Verdict: allocation mismatch, not capital scarcity — a global glut concentrated away from early- and growth-stage Canada. Global figures: Preqin / Bain Global PE Report 2026 (Tier 4). Canadian: BDC (Tier 1) · RBCx (Tier 3/4).

Three balance sheets, one diagnosis: households, corporations and the funds themselves all hold capital that does not reach Canadian growth companies. The abundance is real — it is simply pointed elsewhere.

07 The read

A multi-billion-dollar, stage-specific supply gap.

Canada's venture under-investment is real, large (~C$5–11B/yr), and concentrated at the scale-up stage — and what's missing is supplied by US capital that ultimately pulls control and upside south. The lever is to mobilise domestic growth capital and crowd it in, not to compel it.

The gap is the diagnosis. The next question is what the jurisdictions that closed a gap like it actually did — and where Canada sits against them on the numbers.
