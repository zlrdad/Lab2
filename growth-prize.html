02 The living-standards slide

The slide, charted — two honest measures.

The hero figure in full. Two honest measures of the same story. On the living-standards (PPP) measure Canada slid to ~71% of the US in a decade; on the market-exchange-rate measure the slide is steeper still — because Canada is losing ground and its currency has weakened. The two must never be conflated in one chart.

Canadian GDP per capita, as a share of the US

Two measures · 2014/2010 → 2024/2025
How to read it. The solid coloured line is the cleaner living-standards number — lead with it for policy. The grey dashed line is the more dramatic figure the vault has headlined — correct, but partly a currency story, so it is always labelled nominal. Both endpoints also flatter the size of the slide: 2014 sits near Canada's commodity-cycle peak, and part of the 2022–24 leg reflects record population growth diluting the denominator (capital shallowing) — so read the trend, not the endpoints.

03 Where the leverage lives

A sliver of firms moves almost everything.

The gap is not diffuse — it is concentrated in the firms that fail to appear. A vanishingly small share of companies generates the overwhelming majority of new jobs, private R&D and productivity growth. Land those firms — and keep them — and the aggregate numbers move; lose them and no amount of broad-based effort compensates.

Small share of firms, outsized share of the outcome

Share of a named outcome vs share of firms · Canada · US · OECD
The leverage compounds. Each high-tech job supports roughly five more local jobs — about three times the manufacturing multiplier — so the firms that scale (or leave) move far more than their own payroll (Moretti 2012). And the dependence is structural: US startups have been the only firm-age group with positive net job creation in every year for two decades, contributing 142% of net job creation, while firms aged 11+ — 81.6% of employment — were net job destroyers in 11 of 20 years (Looze & Goff 2022). Across 20 OECD economies, without scaling SMEs aggregate productivity would run about 6% lower (OECD 2025).

This is why the productivity gap and the scale-up gap are one problem, not two. Firm size is the dominant driver of Canada's 27-point productivity shortfall — a higher share of activity in small firms, plus a steeper penalty for staying small, together explain about 60% of it (Gu & Lesica 2025). And the gap is concentrated, not broad-based: the Bank of Canada finds the top 10% of earners account for roughly three-quarters of the Canada–US GDP-per-adult gap — consistent, this report argues, with missing US-style "superstar firms" rather than ordinary workers underperforming (MacGee & Rodrigue 2024). The lever is not more firms; it is more firms that scale — and scaling is, in large part, a financing-and-retention problem.

04 The transmission mechanism

Deeper capital markets, faster growth — and the best recent evidence points one way.

If growth companies are the lever, public capital markets are what pulls it. The largest recent test of that link — a World Federation of Exchanges panel of 37 economies over nearly two decades — strengthens the case on a question the older literature left open.

Stock-market capitalisation has a unidirectional, long-run effect that runs one way — from market depth to growth, not the reverse (Granger sense) — concentrated in high-income economies like Canada.

Using second-generation panel methods — FMOLS for the long run and a VECM Granger-causality design for direction — across 37 countries and roughly 78 quarters, Demir finds the market-capitalisation-to-GDP ratio drives growth rather than merely tracking it. For high-income countries the coefficient is 0.45, significant at the 1% level — a 10% deeper market maps to roughly 0.045% higher long-run real growth — while the effect is statistically insignificant in low- and middle-income economies. Canada sits squarely in the high-income panel where the link is robust.

Five channels carry it: mobilising and allocating capital, providing liquidity, aggregating information through price signals, sharing risk, and the wealth effect on demand. One bridge to name honestly: Demir measures market-cap depth, where Canada still scores high — the concern here is composition, a hollowing operating-issuer base and a stalled IPO pipeline that erode the future depth and price-discovery those channels rely on. The corollary is the one that matters for this chapter — a shrinking listed base is not merely a markets problem; it is a growth problem. That is what makes Ontario's exit engine, next, the load-bearing evidence.

Step 02 · KoreaKorea, meanwhile, held its range — 33–159/yr, no secular decline. A peer advanced economy saw no collapse at all.

The gap is a scale-up problem; the scale-up problem is a capital-markets problem.

That is why fixing how Ontario finances and retains its growth companies is one of the highest-return economic policies available.

06 The financing gap, sized

Under-capitalised where it counts.

Canada's under-investment in productive capital is broad — business investment per worker in 2023 stood at just 85% of its 2014 level while the US rose 21% over the same decade (OECD 2025). But the cleanest apples-to-apples measure of the growth-financing gap is venture-capital investment as a share of GDP. Canada sits well below the comparator median and less than half the US level — the shortfall concentrated at Series B+, exactly where scale-ups are made or lost.

Venture capital as a share of GDP

Canada vs comparator median vs US · 2024 (OECD market statistics)
Closing to the comparator median (0.355%) implies ≈ C$3.7–5.1B/yr of additional venture capital; closing to the US level (0.53%) implies ≈ C$9.4–10.8B/yr. The shortfall is THE primary gap — Series B+ scale-up financing. (On the alternative Compendium basis Canada is ~0.35%, nearer the median; the dollar-gap arithmetic here uses the OECD basis — the two are never mixed.)

And the pipeline increasingly builds elsewhere.

The financing gap is not an abstraction — Canadian-led founders increasingly choose to build outside Canada. Only 32.4% of Canadian-led high-potential startups from the 2024 cohort were headquartered here — a VC-downturn year — down from more than 67% across the 2015–2019 bull-market average, with nearly half of the 2024 cohort now US-based.

07 What's at stake
>US$0B
identified value leakage · ~75 departed or acquired champions

Across roughly seventy-five companies that were built here and then relocated, sold early, or lost at scale, the vault's Value Leakage Index tallies more than US$680 billion of enterprise value no longer anchored in Canada — alongside an estimated ~C$1.5B a year in forgone tax from just the top twenty (a conservative floor of C$750M/yr excluding the most uncertain category). This is a vault-tally at scale — an internal sum across case studies, not a single published statistic — and it is the mirror image of the prize: the cost of the status quo, growing with each champion lost while the scale-up and retention gaps stay open.

07b What winning is worth

The prize, sized — the mirror image of the leak.

Leakage is the cost of the status quo; the prize is what reversing it is worth. Closing even a quarter of the living-standards gap this report diagnoses — a deliberately conservative fraction — would add on the order of C$4,000–5,000 per person, roughly C$10,000 a household, in today's dollars. And it is not the only prize on the table.
