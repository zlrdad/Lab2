◇ Chapter · the growth sectors

Nine sectors. One capital-markets lens.

Ontario's nine growth sectors are where capital-formation policy is most likely to pay off. But the national accounts show where the money actually goes — and in 2025, for every dollar of business investment in intellectual property, Ontario's capital formation put C$2.76 into residential structures. The productive-capital story starts here.

the composition problem
01 Structures vs intangibles

Ontario's capital mix tilts to structures over intangibles.

This is the composition problem that sits underneath every sector page. The Ontario Economic Accounts split business fixed investment into four lines; two of them — intellectual-property products (software, R&D, mineral exploration, design) and machinery & equipment — are the productive capital that scales a Cohere or a Shopify. The largest line by far is residential structures — which national-accounts convention books to the business sector, though it is overwhelmingly household- and homebuilder-driven housing demand rather than a growth-firm capital-allocation choice. Read the gap as a composition fact about the province's capital mix, not as evidence that a Cohere or Shopify picked buildings over R&D; the intellectual-property and machinery lines are the productive-capital signal to watch. 02 The real economy

Where Ontario's output actually sits.

Before mapping the growth sectors, ground them in the macro data. Here is Ontario's real GDP by industry in 2025 (chained 2017 dollars), with the change since 2019 beside each bar. The pattern is stark: the services and knowledge industries are growing — Finance & Insurance is up 28%, Professional services up 20%, Information & Culture (tech and telecom) up 16% — while the goods-producing base is shrinking, with Manufacturing down 7% and auto output down 12%. 04 Sectors → boards

Where each sector lists — and where it flees.

The capital-markets lens sharpens when you ask which board each sector actually lists on. The answer sorts the nine sectors into four very different venues: the senior TSX carries the banks and the one tech giant; the junior boards run on mining; and Ontario's scaled deep-tech increasingly skips all three for Nasdaq.

TSX
senior · scale & capital
Financial Services leads by value (~C$1.9T, 63 issuers). Mining leads by count (181). The lone scaled tech name is Shopify. This is where a champion should list — and rarely does.
TSXV
venture · juniors
Mining is the board — 899 of 1,347 issuers (67%). The two biggest tech names, Topicus and Lumine, are Constellation spin-offs. Median issuer: nano-cap C$17M.
CSE
lighter regime
Mining again (340 of 601, 57%). Its largest Life Sciences names are US cannabis operators listing here for the regime, not for Ontario capital. 89% of equities are under C$50M.
Nasdaq & US
the flight destination
Scaled deep tech skips Canada's boards: Xanadu (quantum) went public via US SPAC; Tenstorrent re-domiciled its HQ to the US. Where the mid-cap champion tier should be, there is a gap.
The mismatch. Ontario's fastest-growing industries — finance, technology, professional services — have almost no representation on the venture boards, while mining dominates all three domestic venues. The sectors the growth agenda cares most about are the ones with the weakest public-market home.

05·b Recent listings

Ten true IPOs in twenty-four months.

Strip out the ETF and fund launches that dominate the raw “new listings” tally and the count of true operating-company IPOs sits near a multi-year low: across the two years to May 2026 there were just 6 genuine operating-company IPOs on the TSX and 4 on the TSXV — a thin count against the 44 new TSX listings of the 2021 peak (WFE), and one that deliberately excludes the TSXV→TSX graduations, RTOs and qualifying transactions counted separately below. Only one — Metatek, a technology name — landed in a mapped growth sector; the rest were mining, consumer and real estate. This is the sector-level face of the IPO drought.

06 The read

Great sectors, mis-wired capital.

Read across the nine sectors and one pattern repeats: Ontario has world-class research and genuine sectoral strength, but the capital that scales it is either thin in productive intangibles, arriving from foreign investors, or routing through FDI and grants rather than domestic equity markets. The productive-capital mix (the 2.76× residential-to-IP composition gap), the industry divergence (knowledge up, goods down), and the board mismatch (champions with no domestic home) are three views of the same problem. The next chapters diagnose it.
