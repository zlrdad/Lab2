Nine sectors. One capital-markets lens.

Ontario's nine growth sectors are where capital-formation policy is most likely to pay off. But the national accounts show where the money actually goes — and in 2025, for every dollar Ontario businesses invested in intellectual property, they poured C$2.76 into housing. The productive-capital story starts here.

the composition problem
01 Buildings over ideas

Ontario invests in structures, not intangibles.

This is the composition problem that sits underneath every sector page. The Ontario Economic Accounts split business fixed investment into four lines; two of them — intellectual-property products (software, R&D, mineral exploration, design) and machinery & equipment — are the productive capital that scales a Cohere or a Shopify. The largest line by far is residential structures. Switch the view below between the raw levels and the housing-to-IP ratio over time. Nominal C$ billions, 1997-onward series; 2000–2025 shown.

The read: In 2025 Ontario businesses invested C$95.0B in residential structures against just C$34.4B in intellectual property and C$37.4B in machinery & equipment. Even taking IP and M&E together (C$71.8B), housing alone is larger. The 4.5× housing-to-IP peak of 2021 has narrowed to 2.76× — progress, but the capital mix still favours buildings over the intangibles that scale the growth sectors.

02 The real economy

Where Ontario's output actually sits.

Before mapping the growth sectors, ground them in the macro data. Here is Ontario's real GDP by industry in 2025 (chained 2017 dollars), with the change since 2019 beside each bar. The pattern is stark: the services and knowledge industries are growing — Finance & Insurance is up 28%, Professional services up 20%, Information & Culture (tech and telecom) up 16% — while the goods-producing base is shrinking, with Manufacturing down 7% and auto output down 12%. 04 Sectors → boards

Where each sector lists — and where it flees.

The capital-markets lens sharpens when you ask which board each sector actually lists on. The answer sorts the nine sectors into four very different venues: the senior TSX carries the banks and the one tech giant; the junior boards run on mining; and Ontario's scaled deep-tech increasingly skips all three for Nasdaq.

TSX
senior · scale & capital
Financial Services leads by value (~C$1.9T, 63 issuers). Mining leads by count (181). The lone scaled tech name is Shopify. This is where a champion should list — and rarely does.
TSXV
venture · juniors
Mining is the board — 899 of 1,347 issuers (67%). The two biggest tech names, Topicus and Lumine, are Constellation spin-offs. Median issuer: nano-cap C$17M.
CSE
lighter regime
Mining again (340 of 601, 57%). Its largest Life Sciences names are US cannabis operators listing here for the regime, not for Ontario capital. 89% of equities are under C$50M.
Nasdaq & US
the flight destination
Scaled deep tech skips Canada's boards: Xanadu (quantum) went public via US SPAC; Tenstorrent re-domiciled its HQ to the US. Where the mid-cap champion tier should be, there is a gap.
The mismatch. Ontario's fastest-growing industries — finance, technology, professional services — have almost no representation on the venture boards, while mining dominates all three domestic venues. The sectors the growth agenda cares most about are the ones with the weakest public-market home.

05 The read

Great sectors, mis-wired capital.

Read across the nine sectors and one pattern repeats: Ontario has world-class research and genuine sectoral strength, but the capital that scales it is either going into buildings instead of intangibles, arriving from foreign investors, or routing through FDI and grants rather than domestic equity markets. The productive-capital mix (2.76× housing over IP), the industry divergence (knowledge up, goods down), and the board mismatch (champions with no domestic home) are three views of the same problem.
