◇ Prescription · the levers

Four levers to build, scale, and keep Canadian champions.

The levers follow directly from the diagnosis — under-investment at the scale-up stage, exit by acquisition or US listing, and thin liquidity — and from what works abroad. They lead with incentives, not mandates: the aim is to make backing, scaling, and keeping Canadian companies the rational private choice, not a compelled one.

01 Lever ① · supply of growth capital tests H-S1 / H-S2

A Canadian Growth Capital Compact.

The scale-up rounds Canada won't write are the binding supply constraint. The fix is an anchor fund-of-funds at market terms, with the matching, sweetener, and prudential carve-outs that crowd private money in — not a mandate that pushes it out.

Make the Growth VCCI permanent and scale it to C$3–5B — an anchor fund-of-funds deploying at market terms, on the KVIC (Korea), Tesi (Finland) and NZ Growth Fund model. Four design bolts make it work:

0.3 : 1 government match
crowd-in, not subsidy
A co-investment match on institutional and insurer commitments — concessional enough to move capital, lean enough not to subsidise it.
Yozma-style sweetener
Israel · 1993
A buyout-at-cost + 5–7% / 5yr call option on the public stake (Israel's Yozma) — caps government upside, hands the winners to private GPs.
≥ 1× "reinvest in Canada" clause
Korea Global Fund
A contractual condition that foreign VCs accessing the FoF redeploy at least 1× of drawn capital domestically — the Korea Global Fund mechanism.
OSFI LICAT carve-out
insurer balance sheets
Cut the 40% capital factor toward 30% for qualifying domestic VC — mirroring Canada's own July-2025 infrastructure carve-out, freeing insurer balance sheets.
At VCCI's realised ~3:1 leverage, a C$3–5B FoF deploying ~C$1B/yr mobilises ~C$4B/yr total — about the size of the median scale-up gap.
Mandate verdict: against a hard pension mandate. Cumming calls it "a tax on pensioners"; the UK's Mansion House pledge is running well short of its allocation goal — 0.6% unlisted-equity exposure against its 5% UK-assets sub-target. Make domestic VC the rational choice instead.
Confidence, graded honestly: the fund-of-funds leverage is a B-grade estimate scaled from VCCI; the pension-mobilisation piece is softer — C-grade and inferential, gated on the flagged pension-allocation data gap. The compact is the right posture; its realised flow can't yet be costed as precisely as the FoF.

02 Lever ② · demand-side capital tests H-C1

A Canadian Innovation Investment tax scheme.

A SEIS/EIS-style investor credit — roughly ~50% at seed, ~30% at growth — pulls private angel and early-growth money into qualifying Canadian companies. The UK's design is proven; copy it rather than reinvent it.

Copy the proven SEIS design: high-rate relief, a tight seed band, a risk-to-capital screen, a domestic-content requirement, and evaluation built in from day one. Avoid a broad patent box — it leaks to incumbents and rewards activity that would have happened anyway.

At ~C$0.43 per gross dollar mobilised, the scheme sits below the mining flow-through envelope Canada already funds every year — a costed, precedented ask, not a leap.

03 Lever ③ · retention tests H-R1 / R2 / R3 / R4 / R6

Retention architecture.

Supplying capital isn't enough if champions are sold or relisted abroad the moment they scale. Five interlocking pieces keep pre-IPO companies — and their founders, IP, HQ and hiring — anchored in Canada.

Pre-IPO liquidity venue
PISCES · LTAF
A PISCES-style private-secondary market plus a Canadian LTAF — letting founders and early employees realise liquidity without a sale or a US listing. The PISCES venue sits squarely inside the OSC's own Goal-5 pilot-program mandate (below).
Employee-option reform
BSPCE · France / QESO · Sweden
Codify qualifying private-company options taxed at capital rates, at sale not exercise — killing the "dry tax on illiquid shares" (France's BSPCE, Sweden's QESO) so founders and early staff can hold equity and stay. Framed honestly as friction removal: vesting, not the tax rate, is the proven retention lever. Canada's 2024 option restrictions were already cancelled (Mar 2025), so the task is codification, not reversal.
Retention conditionality
Quebec CQCP · Israel §19A
Attach Canadian-HQ / IP / hiring conditions to public grants — à la Quebec's CQCP and Israel's §19A IP-transfer levy.
Governance & control
necessary, not sufficient
Benefit-company form, facilitative dual-class with sunset-renewal, and steward-ownership structures. Necessary but not sufficient on their own.
Anti-take-private scoping
CUSMA-constrained
Scope measures against opportunistic take-privates — design-constrained by CUSMA and Canada's investment treaties, so calibrate carefully.
Governance tools alone don't keep a company home. Retention works only as an architecture — liquidity, conditionality, control and defence reinforcing one another.

04 Lever ④ · liquidity & coverage tests H-S3 + H-C2

Fix the trading and the research.

The diagnosis identified turnover velocity as the binding public-market constraint. The cleanest causal fixes are designated market-making, mandated coverage, and a retail demand base routed at domestic growth equity.

STAR-style market-maker + coverage
Borsa Italiana
A Borsa Italiana STAR-style designated-market-maker and mandated analyst-coverage obligation for the TSXV / CSE — the cleanest causal liquidity fix in the evidence.
RRSP / TFSA qualifying-investment routes
PEA-PME template · ISK cautionary
Expand RRSP/TFSA qualifying investments to admit domestic growth equity and VC/PE, routing household capital home — on France's PEA-PME domestic-content template. Sweden's ISK proves retail accounts can reach mass adoption but is the cautionary case: low additionality, regressive, no domestic rule — and the evidence it lifts market liquidity is thin and circumstantial.
Graduation escalator
TSXV → TSX
Reinforce the TSXV → TSX graduation escalator so junior-board winners have a clear, low-friction path up to the senior board — operationalising the matrix guardrail that the junior board's job is graduation quality, not listing count.
The STAR market-maker and coverage obligation route through the CSA–OSC — and now sit inside the regulator's own remit. The OSC Strategic Plan 2024–2030 makes capital formation a statutory mandate item (housed in the Office of Economic Growth and Innovation) and commits Goal 5 to "pilot programs in support of novel capital formation methods," with Goal 3 to right-size regulation. So STAR and the PISCES venue (Lever ③) read as delivering the OSC's own stated goals, not external asks — lowering the institutional friction. (Strategic intent is not yet committed rule-making.)

05 Situation → lever

Which lever fits which company.

The levers aren't a menu — they're matched to the situation a champion is actually in. Six recurring profiles, each pointing to a different combination.

Independent at IPO-candidate scale
Cohere · Wealthsimple
PISCES / LTAF + TSX-listing incentives + the FoF — keep them liquid and home until they list.
Acquisition-in-play
Tenstorrent
Anti-take-private scoping + retention conditionality — raise the friction on an opportunistic sale.
Growth scaling on US capital
Clio · Ada · Tailscale
FoF + match + tax scheme to crowd domestic capital into rounds US investors currently own.
Domestic-capital exemplar
StackAdapt · Neo
Scale it / make it routine — these are proof the model works; the job is to make them ordinary.
Foreign-PE-controlled mature
Fullscript · PointClickCare
Intervene earlier — the leverage is in the growth round before control passes offshore.
Public, depressed, takeover-exposed
Coveo · D2L
Anti-take-private + liquidity measures — thin trading is what makes them cheap to take.

07 The objective

Make the exception the rule.

The goal is to make the retention exceptions — StackAdapt, Wealthsimple, MDA — the rule.

Each company that stayed, scaled and listed at home did so against the grain of the system. The four levers are an attempt to bend the system the other way — so that staying is the default, not the act of defiance.
